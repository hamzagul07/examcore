The strongest IB Chemistry Extended Essay topic is a **quantitative investigation with a measurable chemical variable** — a lab-based study you can run safely with school apparatus, or a database study where the analysis carries the marks. The chemistry has to be front and centre: mechanisms, quantities, energetics, kinetics or equilibrium — not a cooking experiment or a biology question wearing a lab coat. Your research question should name one thing you change and one chemical property you measure, connect to underlying chemistry you can argue from, and clear a proper **hazard and risk assessment**. This post shows how to turn an interest into an examinable RQ, with an example table and the methodology traps that sink EEs. For criteria in full, see the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## Chemistry EE vs Chemistry IA — what's different

The Internal Assessment and the Extended Essay overlap — both are individual investigations tied to chemistry syllabus theory — but the EE is far larger in **scale and independence**. The IA is around ten hours of work and a report of roughly 6–12 pages, marked on five IA criteria. The EE is a **4,000-word** research essay marked on five *EE* criteria (A–E) by an external examiner, and it demands a sustained argument, not just a clean titration table. Where the IA rewards a controlled method and correct significant figures, the EE rewards **critical thinking**: you interpret your data against established chemistry, quantify uncertainty honestly, and reach a reasoned conclusion. If you have already written a Chemistry IA, that is your foundation — the [IB Chemistry IA guide](/blog/ib-chemistry-ia-guide) covers the shared groundwork on variables, uncertainty and evaluation.

## What makes a strong Chemistry EE research question

A good Chemistry EE RQ does five things:

- **Focused** — one independent variable, one dependent chemical variable, one bounded system. Not "what affects reaction rate" but "how does *temperature* affect the *rate* of the sodium thiosulfate–hydrochloric acid reaction".
- **Measurable** — the DV is a chemical quantity: rate, enthalpy change, concentration, pH, percentage yield, absorbance. Numbers you can process with units and uncertainties.
- **Clearly chemical** — the trend is explained by real chemistry (collision theory, Hess's law, Le Chatelier, acid–base equilibria), not just an observation with no mechanism behind it.
- **Feasible and non-trivial** — the method is accessible, *and* the answer isn't already obvious from the textbook. If the result is trivially known, there's nothing to investigate.
- **Safe** — a written **risk assessment** covering hazards, concentrations, ventilation and disposal. No topic that needs reagents or conditions your lab can't handle safely.

## Choosing and narrowing your topic

Start from an area of chemistry you find interesting — that feeds Criterion E (engagement). Then narrow it in three moves. **Pick a measurable property**: rate of reaction, enthalpy of combustion or neutralisation, equilibrium position, titratable acidity, or absorbance via colorimetry. **Choose one variable to change and one to measure**, so "vitamin C in fruit" becomes "effect of storage time on the ascorbic acid concentration of orange juice, by redox titration". Finally, **set a range and controls** — about five levels of the IV, three or more repeats, and a written list of the variables you hold constant (temperature, concentration, volume, apparatus). A single tightly controlled question reads as rigorous; a broad sweep reads as unfocused.

If a controlled experiment isn't feasible — the reagents are too hazardous, the timescale too long, or the equipment unavailable — a **database or secondary-data study** is legitimate, provided the analysis is strong. Because you're then marked on processing rather than lab technique, you need a real quantitative angle: statistical treatment, a modelled relationship, or a rigorous chemical interpretation. A graph copied from a dataset with no processing will not score.

## Example research questions: too broad to sharpened

Treat these as **starting points**, not copy-paste answers — an RQ identical to an online example undermines authenticity.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
|---|---|
| What affects reaction rate? | How does temperature (20–60 °C) affect the rate of the sodium thiosulfate–hydrochloric acid reaction, measured by time-to-opacity? |
| Which fuel is best? | How does alcohol chain length (C₁–C₅) affect the enthalpy of combustion measured by calorimetry? |
| Is vitamin C good for you? | How does storage time affect the ascorbic acid concentration of orange juice, determined by redox titration? |
| Do antacids work? | How does antacid brand affect the mass of hydrochloric acid neutralised per gram, by back-titration? |
| What affects equilibrium? | How does temperature affect the position of the cobalt(II) chloride equilibrium, measured by colorimetric absorbance? |
| Is hard water a problem? | How does water source affect total calcium ion concentration, determined by EDTA complexometric titration? |

Each sharpened RQ names an IV with a range, a measurable chemical DV with a method, and a bounded system — and each links straight to chemistry you can argue from.

## Methodology and pitfalls

A Chemistry EE lives or dies on **quantitative rigour**. Design for controlled variables first: list every factor that could confound your DV (temperature, concentration, volume, apparatus, timing) and state how you hold each constant. Collect **enough data** — around five IV levels with three-plus repeats — then show **one full sample calculation** with correct units and significant figures before tabulating the rest. Record and propagate **uncertainty** from your apparatus; an EE that ignores error has no basis for evaluation. Use a statistical or graphical treatment (a trend line with a gradient, a rate constant, a percentage-error comparison to a literature value) that the data actually justifies.

The pitfalls examiners see every year:

- **Too broad** — a question needing several investigations, so none is answered in depth.
- **Not really chemistry** — a cooking or biology topic with no chemical mechanism or quantity at its core.
- **Trivial result** — an answer already obvious from the syllabus, leaving nothing to investigate.
- **No accessible method** — a lovely idea with no safe, school-feasible way to measure the DV.
- **Uncertainty ignored** — no error analysis, so the evaluation has nothing to stand on.
- **Descriptive, not argued** — data presented without interpreting it against established chemistry.
- **Safety skipped** — no risk assessment for hazards, concentrations, ventilation or disposal.

## How the Extended Essay is marked

The EE is marked out of roughly **34 marks** across five criteria and graded on an **A–E** scale:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp RQ and a sound, well-justified methodology.
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of the relevant chemistry, used accurately.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis, argument and evaluation — the heaviest lifting.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, formatting, referencing and the word limit.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your **RPPF** (Reflections on Planning and Progress Form).

Your EE grade combines with TOK to award up to **3 bonus diploma points**. Your essay must be supervised by a subject-qualified supervisor, and Chemistry-specific band detail is best read against the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide) and your school's subject brief rather than memorised second-hand.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does my Chemistry EE have to be a lab experiment?

No — a lab-based investigation is ideal because it gives you original, quantitative data, but a well-justified **database or secondary-data study** is allowed and can score highly. The condition is that the analysis is genuinely strong: a real quantitative or statistical treatment, not a graph copied from a dataset.

### How is the Chemistry EE different from the Chemistry IA?

Scale and independence. The IA is around ten hours and 6–12 pages on five IA criteria; the EE is a **4,000-word** essay on five EE criteria (A–E), externally marked, demanding a sustained argument rather than a clean data table. See the [Chemistry IA guide](/blog/ib-chemistry-ia-guide) for the shared foundations.

### What counts as a "chemical enough" topic?

One where a chemical quantity or mechanism is central — rate, enthalpy, equilibrium, concentration, pH, yield — and you can explain the trend with real chemistry. A topic that's mostly cooking or biology, with chemistry only in the background, won't satisfy the examiners.

### How many marks is the Extended Essay worth?

The EE is marked out of roughly **34 marks** across criteria A–E and graded **A–E**. Combined with TOK it can add up to **3 bonus points** to your diploma total.

### How narrow should my research question be?

Narrow enough that one clear variable answers it in depth. "How does temperature affect the rate of the thiosulfate–acid reaction?" works as an EE; "what affects reaction rate?" is a whole topic, not a research question.

## Bottom line

A strong Chemistry Extended Essay starts with a modest, measurable, well-controlled question built on real chemistry — one IV, one chemical DV, a sensible range, real repeats, propagated uncertainty, and an argument you build across 4,000 words. Pick a measurable property and an area you care about, then sharpen it until it fits the *How does X affect Y?* template and clears the hazard and risk assessment.
