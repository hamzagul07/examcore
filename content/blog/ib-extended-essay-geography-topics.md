A strong IB Geography Extended Essay is grounded in **geographic concepts and, ideally, primary fieldwork or data** (or robust secondary spatial data), and it answers a **place- or process-specific research question at an appropriate scale**. The best topic lets you collect or analyse real spatial data — measurements along a transect, a survey across a study area, land-use change over time — and then explain it using a named geographic theory or model. A "good" geography EE question is (1) tied to a specific place or process you can actually investigate, (2) set at a manageable scale, and (3) analytical and evaluative rather than descriptive — so it demands maps, graphs, statistics, and interpretation, not a "everything about my city" report. Get those three right and the analysis and evaluation marks, which decide most grades, open up. This post shows how to choose and sharpen a topic, with an example research-question table you can adapt.

## Geography EE vs the Geography IA

The two are related but distinct. The **Internal Assessment** is a fieldwork report built around a single fieldwork question, collected and written up to a set structure. The **Extended Essay** is a longer, more independent 4,000-word investigation: you set the research question, choose the methods, and sustain an argument grounded in geographic theory. A geography EE ideally uses **primary fieldwork or data you gather yourself**, or robust **secondary spatial data** (census, satellite imagery, official statistics) where fieldwork is impractical. Either way, the essay must be analytical geography — spatial patterns, processes, and their causes — not a travel-guide description of a place.

For the fieldwork-report side, see the [IB Geography IA guide](/blog/ib-geography-ia-guide). This post is only about the essay.

## What a geography EE actually asks you to do

Because geography is a spatial, evidence-based subject, your essay must *investigate*, not narrate. Strong essays usually do one of these:

- **Test a model or theory against a real place** — for example whether a river's characteristics downstream match the Bradshaw model, or whether a settlement's land use fits a classic urban model.
- **Explain a spatial pattern** — why a variable changes across a transect, a coastline, or a catchment.
- **Evaluate the impact of a process** — the effect of an intervention, management scheme, or environmental change on a specific area.
- **Compare two places or two points in time** on a clearly defined geographic measure.

Every one of these ends in analysis and judgement supported by data. A descriptive question — "the geography of my town" — has nothing to analyse and cannot reach the top bands.

## Choosing and narrowing a research question

The commonest geography EE failure is scale: the topic is a whole city or a global issue, so the essay drowns in description. Narrowing means fixing the **place**, the **process or variable**, and the **scale** at which you can actually gather or source data. The table below shows the move from a vague topic to an arguable, data-driven question.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
| --- | --- |
| The geography of coastal erosion | To what extent do longshore drift processes explain sediment size variation along [named] beach? |
| Urbanisation in my city | To what extent does land-use zoning in [named district] fit the concentric-zone model of urban structure? |
| Rivers and their features | How well does the Bradshaw model predict changes in channel width and velocity downstream along [named river]? |
| The impact of tourism | To what extent has tourism development altered land use in [named coastal village] between 2005 and 2025? |
| Climate change effects | To what extent does the urban heat island effect raise temperatures across [named city] on clear summer nights? |
| Deforestation problems | To what extent does distance from a road corridor explain forest-cover loss in [named region], using satellite data? |

The sharpened versions all share a shape: a **command term** ("to what extent", "how well"), a **named place**, a **specific process or variable**, and often a **defined timeframe or scale**. That shape makes the essay measurable and arguable. For how command terms drive marks, see [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained).

## Method: fieldwork, data, and geographic technique

Your methodology should read like a genuine investigation. State your study area, your sampling strategy, the variables you measured or sourced, and any ethical or risk considerations for fieldwork. Strong essays:

- Use **primary fieldwork data** — transects, quadrats, surveys, field measurements — or **robust secondary spatial data** where fieldwork is not feasible.
- **Present data geographically** — annotated maps, graphs, GIS layers, cross-sections, photographs with analysis — every figure titled and interpreted.
- **Apply statistics** where appropriate — correlation, significance tests — to support claims rather than assert them.
- **Link findings to a named geographic model or concept**, and evaluate how well reality fits the theory.
- **Reach a hedged, evidence-based conclusion** that answers the exact question and acknowledges the limits of the data.

## Pitfalls that cost marks

- **Too large a scale.** A whole country or global issue cannot be investigated in 4,000 words. Fix a small, accessible study area.
- **Descriptive, not analytical.** "All about [place]" has no argument. Add a command term and a process to explain.
- **No geographic theory.** Data without a named model or concept has nothing to test against. Anchor the essay to geographic theory.
- **Figures without interpretation.** Maps and graphs must be analysed, titled, and labelled with units — not dropped in as decoration.
- **Ignoring reliability.** Weather, sample size, timing, and secondary-data quality all shape your findings; a top essay evaluates them.
- **Weak spatial dimension.** If the question could be answered without reference to place, pattern, or process, it is not really a geography question.

## How the Extended Essay is marked

The EE is externally assessed on **five criteria** worth **34 marks** in total, and it is graded **A–E**:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp, appropriately scaled research question and a clear methodology.
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of the relevant geographic concepts, models, and terminology.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis, evaluation, and a sustained argument built on your data — where geography essays are won or lost.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, mapping and figures, formatting, and consistent academic citation.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your Reflections on Planning and Progress Form (RPPF).

Combined with your Theory of Knowledge grade, the EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus points** to your diploma. The finer marking detail differs by subject and by exam session, so treat the band descriptors as authoritative and confirm current requirements with your supervisor — you need a **supervisor qualified in geography**. ## Frequently asked questions

### Does my Geography Extended Essay need fieldwork?

Ideally yes — a geography EE is strongest when it uses primary fieldwork or data you gather yourself. Where fieldwork is impractical, robust secondary spatial data (census, satellite imagery, official statistics) can work, provided the essay stays analytical and grounded in geographic concepts.

### What makes a good Geography EE research question?

One tied to a specific place or process, set at a manageable scale, and analytical rather than descriptive. It should use a command term such as "to what extent", name the study area, and test a spatial pattern or process against a geographic model.

### How is the Geography EE different from the IA?

The IA is a fieldwork report answering a single fieldwork question to a set structure. The EE is a longer, more independent 4,000-word investigation where you set the question, choose the methods, and sustain a theory-grounded argument — with more scope and more evaluation expected.

### What scale should my study area be?

Small and accessible enough to investigate properly — a beach, a river reach, a district, a village, or a defined transect. If you cannot collect or source enough data on it, the scale is too large; narrow it until you can.

### Does my supervisor have to teach Geography?

You need a supervisor qualified in the subject of your essay. For a geography EE that means someone able to guide you on geographic models, fieldwork technique, data presentation, and academic conventions.

## Bottom line

A strong IB Geography Extended Essay is a real investigation of a specific place or process, built on fieldwork or spatial data and grounded in geographic theory — never a descriptive tour of somewhere you like. Fix a small study area, sharpen it into an evaluative question with a command term, present your data properly, and test reality against a named model. Lock the question in early, then follow the [IB Extended Essay guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide) to turn it into a top-band, A-grade essay.
