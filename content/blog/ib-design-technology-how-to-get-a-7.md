A 7 in IB Design Technology comes from two things done well: writing precise, technical answers on the exam papers, and running a genuinely rigorous **design project** for the internal assessment. There is no memorisation shortcut — the mark is earned by matching what each assessment component explicitly rewards, from correct human-factors terminology to a design cycle you actually follow rather than describe. This guide shows how Papers 1, 2 and 3 are marked, how the IA is judged, and where the marks that separate a 5 from a 7 really sit.

## What a 7 actually takes
Grade boundaries shift slightly each session, so chasing an exact percentage is the wrong instinct. What matters is thinking like an examiner. On the written papers, structured questions are marked point-by-point against clear expectations, and extended-response and IA work is judged against **markbands** — level descriptors the examiner uses to place your work in the band it fits, then fine-tune within it. A single sharp insight does not lift a vague answer into the top band; consistent top-band behaviour across the whole response does.

Design Technology sits in **Group 4** as an experimental science, and it blends two things most subjects keep apart: **design creativity** and **applied science and maths**. You reason about materials, structures, mechanics, ergonomics and sustainability with precise terminology — and then apply the **design cycle** to real problems. The syllabus spans design thinking end-to-end: human factors and ergonomics, materials, production methods, sustainability and innovation. Learning to read [IB markbands](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) like an examiner is the highest-leverage habit in the subject, and top candidates move fluently between the science and the design across all of it.

## The three papers and exactly how each is marked
Design Technology is assessed by external papers plus the internal assessment. **SL sits Paper 1 and Paper 2. HL adds Paper 3.**

| Paper | Format | How it's marked | Where 7s are won |
|-------|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Paper 1 | Multiple-choice (core) | One mark per correct answer | Breadth of accurate recall across the core |
| Paper 2 | Structured / data-response (core) | Point-per-mark plus short markband parts | Precise terminology + reading the data/stimulus |
| Paper 3 (HL only) | Extended / options paper (HL topics) | Markbands on extended answers | Depth on HL-only topics, developed argument |

**Paper 1** is multiple-choice on the core. It rewards **breadth of accurate recall** — you cannot bluff it, so the marks go to students who genuinely know their materials, production methods, human factors and design-cycle concepts. Careless misreading of the stem is the usual leak here, not lack of knowledge.

**Paper 2** is structured and data-response on the core, and this is where written technique matters most. Questions range from short *state/outline* parts to longer *explain/discuss* parts, often built around a stimulus — a product, a diagram, a table of material properties, or a scenario. The marks reward **precise technical terminology** and **explicit use of the data**: name the actual property, quote the figure, and refer to the specific product feature rather than writing generically.

**Paper 3** is **HL only** and covers the HL-only topics in more depth (things like classic design, human-factors depth, innovation and invention, commercial production, and sustainability — check your current subject guide for the exact options). It uses extended-response markbands, so the top band needs a **developed, well-structured argument**, not a list of facts. See how the papers differ by level in [IB Design Technology SL vs HL](/blog/ib-design-technology-sl-vs-hl).

## Winning Paper 2 with precise terminology
This is the single biggest differentiator on the written side between a 5 and a 7. Two students can "know" the same topic and score very differently because one writes the exact term examiners want and the other writes a fuzzy paraphrase. For the higher-mark parts, three habits are non-negotiable:

- **Name the property or principle precisely.** Not "it's strong" but the specific property — tensile strength, hardness, toughness, elasticity, stiffness — used correctly for the context. Not "it fits the hand" but the ergonomic or anthropometric reasoning, referencing percentile ranges or the relevant human-factors principle.
- **Apply to the stimulus, not in the abstract.** When there is a product or data set, ground every point in it: this material was chosen *for this feature because of this property*. Generic textbook recall that ignores the stimulus stalls in the middle bands.
- **Match the command term.** *State* and *outline* want brief, distinct points; *explain* wants a reasoned chain; *discuss* and *evaluate* want balance and a judgement. Answer the wrong command term and even correct content loses marks — study [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained) so each verb triggers the right response.

Drill these against real questions from the [SL past papers and topic practice](/ib/past-papers/design-technology-sl) or the [HL past papers](/ib/past-papers/design-technology-hl), then mark honestly against the expected points.

## Nailing the design-project IA
The internal assessment **is the design project**, and it carries real, accessible marks too many students leave until the last minute. It asks you to apply the **design cycle** to a genuine problem: **analyse** a real need, **develop** and **model or prototype** solutions, then **test and evaluate** against your own specification. The same style of major coursework runs at both levels — HL simply expects greater depth.

What separates a top-band IA is not the prettiest render — it is evidence that you *ran* the cycle rather than describing it:

- **A real, well-analysed problem.** Start from an authentic user and need, with proper research (human factors, existing products, constraints) feeding a specific, measurable **design specification**.
- **Genuine development, not one idea.** Show a range of solutions, model and iterate them, and justify decisions against the specification and the science (materials, ergonomics, production feasibility).
- **Real testing and honest evaluation.** Test the prototype against your specification criteria, report what actually happened, and evaluate objectively — including what failed and what you would change.

Sketching and CAD skills help you communicate, but they are not the whole grade; the marks follow the *rigour of the design thinking*. For the wider criterion-marked-coursework mindset, read the [IB internal assessment complete guide](/blog/ib-internal-assessment-complete-guide). Start early — the IA rewards iteration, and iteration takes time.

## HL Paper 3 technique
Paper 3 is HL only and goes deeper on the HL-only topics. Because it is extended-response, it rewards **structured argument** over fact-dumping. Plan before you write: identify the command term, state your line, develop it with technical reasoning (real materials, real production methods, real human-factors or sustainability principles), and where the task asks for evaluation, weigh trade-offs and land a justified judgement. Practise past Paper 3 questions slowly at first, marking against the band descriptors, then to time. The free [HL course](/ib/courses/design-technology-hl) links each HL topic to worked lessons and practice.

## Common mistakes that cap you at a 5
- **Vague, non-technical language.** "Strong," "comfortable," "good design" score little; named properties and principles score.
- **Ignoring the stimulus in Paper 2** — losing every application mark tied to the product or data.
- **Misreading the command term** — writing an explanation when the task says *discuss* or *evaluate*.
- **A described IA, not a run one** — one idea, no real testing, evaluation that just says "it went well."
- **Leaving the design project late** — killing the iteration the top band rewards.
- **Paper 1 carelessness** — losing multiple-choice marks to misread stems, not missing knowledge.
- **HL Paper 3 as a list** — facts with no developed argument stall below the top band.

## A weekly LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED study system
Turn revision into a repeatable loop rather than passive rereading:

1. **LEARN** — take one core topic (a material family, a production method, a human-factors principle) and lock the precise terminology and the underlying science. Work syllabus-by-syllabus with the free [Design Technology SL course](/ib/courses/design-technology-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/design-technology-hl).
2. **PRACTICE** — do a timed question on that topic: a Paper 2 structured part, or (HL) a Paper 3 extended answer. Plan first, then write to time.
3. **GET-MARKED** — mark it against the expected points and band descriptors, then [get an answer marked](/mark?subject=ib-design-technology-hl) for a second opinion aligned to the criteria. Keep a mistake log of your top three recurring errors and drill those first next week.

Run this across the whole core, weighting the topics where your marking is weakest, and keep the **design project** moving in parallel from early in the year.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a 7 harder at HL than SL?
The core written skills and the design-project style are the same, but HL adds Paper 3, HL-only topics, and greater expected depth — including a more sophisticated IA. HL 7s need that extra depth on top of top-band core technique.

### How important is technical terminology?
Decisive on Paper 2 and Paper 3. Named properties, principles and processes score; vague paraphrases do not. Build a precise vocabulary for materials, human factors, production and sustainability and use it deliberately.

### Do I need to be good at drawing or CAD?
It helps you communicate ideas in the design project, but it is not the whole grade. The marks follow the rigour of your design thinking — analysis, development, testing and evaluation — far more than render quality.

### How is the IA marked?
As a design project against criteria that reward genuinely applying the design cycle: a well-analysed real problem, iterative development and modelling, and honest testing and evaluation against your specification. See the [IB internal assessment complete guide](/blog/ib-internal-assessment-complete-guide).

### Is Paper 1 easy because it's multiple-choice?
Not really — you cannot bluff it. It rewards broad, accurate recall across the core, and the usual marks lost are to careless stem-reading, not gaps in knowledge.

## Bottom line
A 7 in IB Design Technology is predictable, not lucky. Learn the core science and terminology topic by topic, practise Paper 2 and (for HL) Paper 3 to time, and mark honestly against the expected points and bands. In parallel, run the **design project** as a real design cycle — analyse, develop, model, test, evaluate — starting early so it can iterate. Cycle LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED every week, drill your recurring errors, and you turn a 7 from a hope into a habit.
