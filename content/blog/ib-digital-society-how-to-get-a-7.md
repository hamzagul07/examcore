A 7 in IB Digital Society comes from applying the course **concepts** to **specific, real-world examples** — analysing how a named digital system affects real people and societies, weighing impacts across stakeholders, and evaluating the ethics and interventions involved. It is not rewarded for describing technology or offering unevidenced opinion. This guide shows what the markbands actually reward and where the marks that separate a 5 from a 7 sit. Because Digital Society is a **new subject** (first assessment 2024, replacing ITGS), always cross-check the exact assessment structure against the current IB subject guide and your teacher.

## What a 7 actually takes
Grade boundaries move each session, so chase the **markbands**, not a percentage. Extended responses are placed in the level descriptor your answer fits, then fine-tuned within it. One sharp paragraph does not lift a descriptive answer into the top band; consistent top-band behaviour across the whole response does. Learning to read [IB markbands](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) like an examiner is the highest-leverage habit in the subject.

The whole course runs on an inquiry framework often called the **CCC model — concepts, content, and contexts**. Concepts include ideas such as **change, expression, identity, power, space, systems,** and **values and ethics**; contexts include the **cultural, economic, environmental, health, human-knowledge, political,** and **social**. A 7 candidate moves fluently between a concept, a real example, and the context it sits in — rather than treating the technology as the point. Preview the framework in the free [Digital Society SL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-hl).

## The assessment and how each part is marked
Digital Society is assessed by external papers plus the internal **Inquiry Project**. State this at a stable level and confirm the exact paper numbering, durations, and weightings in the current subject guide — as a new subject, these details are the ones most worth verifying rather than trusting from memory.

- **Paper 1** — taken at **both SL and HL**. Source- and stimulus-based: you apply course concepts and contexts to real examples in response to the material provided. The reward is analysis grounded in the stimulus, not a pre-learned essay.
- **Paper 2** — taken at **both SL and HL**. Also source/stimulus-based, asking you to apply concepts to real digital-society situations and stakeholders.
- **HL extension** — **HL only**. An additional component focused on the **challenges and interventions** of digital society, based on a **pre-released stimulus**. It rewards deeper synthesis and evaluation of possible responses to a real-world challenge.
- **Inquiry Project (internal assessment)** — a **student-led investigation** into a real-world digital-society example, presented as a multimodal or written report. It is criterion-marked and represents accessible marks too many students leave late.

Whatever the exact format, the through-line is constant: apply concepts to **specific real examples**, analyse impacts on **real stakeholders**, and **evaluate** rather than describe.

## Applying concepts to real examples — the core skill
This is the single biggest differentiator. Weaker answers describe a technology ("AI can recognise faces…"); top answers run it through the framework: *which concept* (say, power or identity), *acting on whom* (named users, groups, institutions), *in which context* (political, health, social), and *with what impact*. Two non-negotiables for the top band:

- **A specific, named example.** Not "some social media platforms" but a particular system, policy, event, or case you actually use to advance the point. One precise, well-developed example beats three vague gestures.
- **Concept-led analysis.** Anchor the example to a course concept and follow the consequence through, rather than listing features of the technology.

Because *command terms* set the task, misreading "evaluate" or "analyse" as "describe" is fatal — study [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained) so each verb triggers the right response. The same essay discipline is drilled in [hitting the top essay markbands](/blog/ib-how-to-hit-the-top-markbands-in-essays).

## Balanced analysis of impacts and stakeholders
Digital systems rarely affect everyone the same way, and the markbands reward candidates who show that. For any example, ask **who benefits, who is harmed, and who decides** — users, developers, governments, marginalised groups, future generations. A 7 answer weighs impacts across at least two contexts (say, economic *and* social) and acknowledges trade-offs rather than presenting a one-sided story. "It is good/bad" is not analysis; "it advantages X while shifting risk onto Y, because…" is.

## Evaluating interventions and ethics
Top-band responses do not stop at impact — they evaluate the **interventions** and **ethics**. What could regulators, designers, or communities do? What are the limits and side-effects of each response? Whose values are encoded in the system, and is that justifiable? Grounding this in the course's ethics concept, and in a real intervention (a law, a design change, a platform policy), is exactly what the HL extension probes and what lifts SL answers into the top band too. Avoid unevidenced moralising — every ethical claim should attach to a specific example and a reasoned line of argument.

## Common mistakes that cap you at a 5
- **Describing the technology** instead of analysing its impact through a concept.
- **Vague or missing examples** — "people" and "society" in general, with no named case.
- **One-sided impact claims** that ignore stakeholders who lose out.
- **Opinion without evidence** — asserting something is harmful without grounding it.
- **Ignoring the stimulus/source** in the papers and writing a generic essay.
- **Misreading the command term** — describing when the task says analyse or evaluate.
- **Leaving the Inquiry Project late**, sacrificing criterion marks that were there for the taking.

## A weekly LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED study system
Turn revision into a repeatable loop rather than passive rereading:

1. **LEARN** — take one concept (say, power or identity) and study how it plays out across two contexts, building a small bank of **specific real examples** you can deploy. Work concept-by-concept through the free [Digital Society SL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-hl).
2. **PRACTICE** — do a timed source-based question, applying concepts to the stimulus. HL students should practise the pre-released-stimulus style for the extension. Build the habit with [Digital Society SL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/digital-society-sl) or [HL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/digital-society-hl).
3. **GET-MARKED** — mark it against the band descriptors, then [get an answer marked](/mark?subject=ib-digital-society-hl) for a second opinion aligned to the criteria. Keep a mistake log of your top three recurring errors and drill those first next week.

Run this cycle across the concepts and contexts, weighting the ones where your band marking is weakest, and start the Inquiry Project early. For the wider diploma picture, see [how to get a 7 across the IB Diploma](/blog/ib-how-to-get-a-7-diploma).

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a 7 harder at HL than SL?
The core skill — applying concepts to real examples — is the same, but HL adds the **HL extension** (challenges and interventions from a pre-released stimulus) and expects greater breadth and synthesis. HL 7s need that deeper evaluation on top of solid Paper 1 and Paper 2 technique.

### How important are real-world examples?
Decisive. The markbands reward analysis anchored to **specific, named examples**; generic references to "technology" or "society" cap you well below the top band. Build a small bank of precise cases you can apply across concepts.

### What is the Inquiry Project?
It is the internal assessment — a student-led investigation into a real digital-society example, presented as a multimodal or written report and marked against criteria. Check the current subject guide and your teacher for the exact requirements, and start early.

### Do I need to describe how the technology works?
Only as much as the analysis needs. Marks come from applying a **concept** to the technology's **impact** on real people and contexts, and from evaluating interventions and ethics — not from technical description for its own sake.

### How much does evaluation really matter?
It is a main separator between a 5 and a 7. Balanced analysis of impacts and stakeholders, plus reasoned evaluation of interventions and ethics, is what pushes an answer into the top band. See [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained).

## Bottom line
A 7 in IB Digital Society is earned by applying the **concepts** to **specific real-world examples**, weighing impacts across stakeholders and contexts, and evaluating interventions and ethics — never by describing technology or asserting opinion. Learn concept-by-concept, practise source-based questions to time (and the HL extension if you are at Higher Level), mark honestly against the bands, and start the Inquiry Project early. Because the subject is new, confirm the exact assessment details in the current IB subject guide — then cycle LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED until top-band analysis becomes a habit.
