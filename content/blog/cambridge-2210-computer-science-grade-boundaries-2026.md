The official grade boundaries for the Cambridge O-Level Computer Science (2210) exams in 2026 will only be published on results day, which for the June session is typically around 13 August 2026. These boundaries, known as **grade thresholds**, are the minimum raw marks you need to achieve a certain grade and are determined *after* all papers have been marked. This article explains how the process works and how you can estimate your performance before the official numbers are released.

## What are grade boundaries?
Grade boundaries are the minimum number of raw marks a candidate needs to be awarded a particular grade. For 2210 Computer Science, Cambridge sets these thresholds for each component paper (Paper 1 and Paper 2) as well as for the overall qualification. It is crucial to understand that these are not percentages; they are absolute scores out of the total marks available for a paper. For example, the boundary for an A grade is the lowest mark that demonstrates A-grade level performance, and this mark can change with every exam session.

## How Cambridge sets the 2210 boundaries
Cambridge's primary goal when setting grade boundaries is to maintain a consistent standard from one year to the next. After marking is complete, senior examiners and statisticians analyse student performance across the globe. They use a statistical process combined with expert judgement to determine if a paper was more or less difficult than in previous sessions. If a paper is deemed harder, the grade boundaries will be lowered to ensure you are not disadvantaged; if it is easier, they will be raised. This principle of **comparable outcomes** ensures that a student achieving an A* in 2026 has demonstrated the same level of knowledge and skill as a student who achieved an A* in a previous year.

## Understanding the 2210 papers and marking
The 2210 syllabus is assessed through two written papers: Paper 1 (Theory) and Paper 2 (Problem-Solving and Programming). Paper 1 covers topics like data representation, hardware, and security, while Paper 2 tests your ability to design, write, and trace algorithms using pseudocode and flowcharts. The marking for both papers is highly specific; you earn marks for using precise technical terms and for providing complete, logical steps in your algorithms. Your final **raw mark** is simply the total of all the points you score, and it is this raw mark that is compared against the grade thresholds.

## When will the 2026 grade boundaries be released?
The definitive grade boundaries for the June 2026 examination series will be published by Cambridge on results day, expected to be on or around 13 August 2026. They are released simultaneously to schools and students worldwide. The reason for this timing is that the boundaries can only be calculated after all the exam scripts have been marked and the overall performance of the cohort has been analysed. Be very wary of any websites or individuals claiming to have 'leaked' or predicted boundaries before this date, as they are purely speculation.

## How to estimate your 2026 grade
While you cannot know the exact 2026 boundaries in advance, you can make a very good estimate of your potential grade. The best method is to practise with recent past papers under timed, exam-like conditions. Once you have completed a paper, you should [mark your paper](/mark) meticulously using the official mark scheme. Then, find the 'Grade Threshold' document for that specific exam session (e.g., June 2024) and compare your raw score to the boundaries for that year. By repeating this process with the last two or three available exam sessions, you can see the typical range for each grade. To simplify this, you can input your scores into a [2210 grade calculator](/tools/grade-boundary-calculator) which uses historical data to provide a helpful estimate.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is there a fixed percentage for an A* in Computer Science?
No, there is no fixed percentage for any grade. Grade boundaries are based on raw marks and are adjusted each year to account for the difficulty of the paper. An A* might require a score equivalent to 88% one year and 92% the next, depending on overall student performance.

### Why did the grade boundaries for my friend's session seem lower/higher?
This is a direct result of Cambridge ensuring fairness across different exam series. If your friend's paper was judged to be more difficult than yours, their grade boundaries would have been set lower. This adjustment ensures that a candidate's final grade reflects their ability, not the relative difficulty of a particular paper.

### Where can I find official past grade boundaries?
Cambridge publishes a 'Grade Threshold' table for every exam session on its public website shortly after results day. For a consolidated view, you can visit the MarkScheme [2210 Computer Science page](/subjects/2210), where we compile historical boundary data to help you easily track trends and analyse past performance requirements.

If you prepare thoroughly, you will be well-placed to achieve your target grade, regardless of where the final boundaries fall.
