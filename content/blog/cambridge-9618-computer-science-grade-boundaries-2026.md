The official grade boundaries for the Cambridge 9618 Computer Science A-Level in 2026 will be published on results day, which for the June session is Thursday 13 August 2026. These boundaries, also known as **grade thresholds**, are the minimum raw marks you need on your papers to achieve a specific grade (A*, A, B, etc.). They are finalised only after all exam papers globally have been marked, so until August 2026, we can only estimate them using data from previous exam sessions.

## What are grade boundaries?
Grade boundaries are the link between the raw mark you score on a paper and the final grade you are awarded. For each component paper (Paper 1, 2, and 3) and for the overall A-Level qualification, Cambridge sets a minimum score for each grade. For example, a boundary shows the lowest raw mark a candidate needs to secure a grade A, the lowest mark for a grade B, and so on. This system ensures that the standard required for a particular grade remains consistent from one year to the next, regardless of the difficulty of the paper.

## How Cambridge determines 9618 thresholds
Setting grade boundaries is a rigorous process designed to ensure fairness. It happens *after* all marking is complete. Senior examiners, who are experts in the 9618 syllabus, analyse a wide range of student scripts and compare them with work from previous years. They use statistical evidence to see how students have performed on the papers overall. Their goal is to ensure that a student who achieves a grade A in 2026 has demonstrated the same level of understanding and skill as a student who achieved a grade A in 2025 or 2024. This is why the boundaries are not, and cannot be, decided before the examination takes place.

## Understanding the 9618 A-Level components
The overall A-Level grade for Computer Science is calculated from your performance across three written papers. Paper 1, 'Theory Fundamentals', covers topics like data representation, computer architecture, networking, and ethics. Paper 2, 'Problem-solving and Programming', is a practical paper testing your ability to design algorithms, write pseudocode, complete trace tables, and apply your knowledge of data structures. Finally, Paper 3, 'Advanced Theory', extends the topics from Paper 1 and introduces advanced concepts such as compilers, artificial intelligence, and computational thinking. Each paper has a specific raw mark total and weighting, and your combined performance determines your final qualification grade.

## Why do grade boundaries change each year?
Grade boundaries for 9618 Computer Science move up or down between exam sessions to account for variations in the difficulty of the examination papers. If, after statistical analysis, Cambridge determines that a paper was slightly more challenging than in the previous year, the grade boundaries will likely be lowered to ensure no student is disadvantaged. Conversely, if a paper is found to be slightly more straightforward, the boundaries may be raised. This adjustment ensures that your final grade is a true reflection of your subject knowledge and ability, not a reflection of the specific paper you happened to sit.
