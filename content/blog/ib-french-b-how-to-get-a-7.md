A 7 in IB French B comes from consistent, examiner-aligned performance across three components — clear communication in your writing, sharp listening and reading under time pressure, and a confident Individual Oral — not from memorising more vocabulary. Language B rewards **message first, then range and accuracy**: the top markband goes to candidates who get their meaning across clearly, match the right text type and register, and control a broad-but-accurate range of language. This guide shows exactly how each component is marked and where the marks that separate a 5 from a 7 actually sit.

## What a 7 actually takes
Grade boundaries shift slightly each session, so chasing an exact percentage is the wrong mindset. What matters is thinking in **markbands**. Your productive writing and your Individual Oral are placed against level descriptors — examiners find the band your performance fits, then fine-tune within it. A single elegant sentence does not lift a muddled task into the top band; consistent top-band behaviour across the whole response does.

So the real question is never "did I use enough clever words?" — it is "which band does this sit in, and what one thing would push it up a level?" Learning to read [IB markbands](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) like an examiner is the highest-leverage habit in the subject. French B is built around **five prescribed themes — identities; experiences; human ingenuity; social organisation; sharing the planet** — and strong candidates can move between them fluently in speech and writing, deploying theme-relevant vocabulary on demand rather than groping for it.

## The components and exactly how each is marked
French B is assessed through two external papers plus an internally-assessed Individual Oral. There is **no written internal assessment** in Language B — the internal component is spoken.

| Component | What it tests | How it's marked | Where 7s are won |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Paper 1 | Productive **writing** — choose one task, produce a text type | Markbands for message, language, and format/register | Matching the text type and register while keeping the message crystal clear |
| Paper 2 | **Receptive** skills — listening + reading comprehension | Points-based comprehension marks | Precise reading of detail; not mishearing under audio pressure |
| Individual Oral (IO) | Spoken performance (internally assessed) | Markbands for communication, language, interaction | Sustaining fluent, developed responses and reacting naturally |

**Paper 1** is productive writing. You choose one task and produce a specific **text type — a blog, an email, an article, a speech, a proposal**, and so on — for a stated purpose, audience, and register. Marks reward getting your message across clearly, using an appropriate range of language accurately, and **choosing the right conventions for the text type**. A grammatically flawless essay written in the wrong register, or an article that reads like a formal letter, is capped below the top band however fluent the French.

**Paper 2** is receptive: listening comprehension plus reading comprehension. It is marked on **comprehension points** — did you understand the specific detail the question asks about? The marks here reward accurate, careful reading and disciplined listening, not writing flair. Losing marks in Paper 2 is usually about missing a detail, mishearing under audio pressure, or being caught by a distractor, not about language production.

**The Individual Oral** is your internally-assessed spoken exam and it differs by level. At **SL** it is based on a **visual stimulus — an image linked to one of the themes** — which you describe and then discuss. At **HL** it is based on an **extract from one of the two literary works** you have studied. Either way it is marked on markbands for communication, command of language, and how well you interact in the follow-up conversation. See the level breakdowns in the [French B SL past papers guide](/blog/ib-french-b-sl-past-papers-guide) and the [HL past papers guide](/blog/ib-french-b-hl-past-papers-guide).

## Winning Paper 1: message, text type, register
This is where a lot of 5s and 6s leak marks that a 7 keeps. Three things are non-negotiable for the top band:

- **A clear, developed message.** Answer the task fully — cover the content the prompt asks for and develop your ideas rather than listing them. Clarity beats risky complexity every time: a sentence the examiner instantly understands scores; a tangled attempt at a subjunctive that obscures your meaning does not.
- **The right text type and its conventions.** A blog has a title and a personal voice; a formal email has a greeting, structure, and sign-off; a proposal argues and recommends. Learn the layout and tone signals for each common text type so you can produce them automatically.
- **Appropriate, consistent register.** Match *tu/vous*, formality, and tone to the audience. Register slips — starting formal and drifting casual — are a classic way to slide out of the top band.

A reliable Paper 1 approach: read the task twice, note purpose/audience/register in the margin, plan the content points, then write to the conventions of the text type. This is exactly the discipline our guide on [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained) reinforces — reading the prompt precisely so you produce what is actually asked for.

## Range and accuracy: clarity beats risk
Examiners reward a **broad range of language used accurately** — varied tenses, connectors, and structures that you actually control. The trap is reaching for complexity you cannot land. A 7 is built on *controlled* range: a handful of well-placed connectors (**cependant, en revanche, par ailleurs, c'est pourquoi**), reliable use of at least two or three tenses, and idiomatic-but-safe phrasing. Watch for **false friends** and for writing in a single tense throughout — both cap accuracy marks. When in doubt, say it clearly in French you own rather than gambling on a structure that might collapse.

## Nailing the Individual Oral
The IO rewards fluency, development, and genuine interaction — not a memorised script. Whether you are describing an image (SL) or discussing a literary extract (HL), practise **speaking continuously** for the required time without long pauses, linking your points, and justifying opinions with reasons and examples. Prepare theme vocabulary so you are never stuck for the word, but let the conversation breathe — the follow-up discussion tests how naturally you react, so listening to the examiner and responding to *their* point scores more than steering back to a rehearsed paragraph. Drill this against the [SL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/french-b-sl) or [HL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/french-b-hl), each linked to a lesson and a criterion-marked task.

## Common mistakes that cap you at a 5
- **Wrong text type or register** in Paper 1 — correct French, wrong format, capped band.
- **Message obscured by risky grammar** — reaching for structures you cannot control.
- **One tense throughout**, or repeated **false friends** — accuracy marks lost.
- **Neglecting listening practice** — Paper 2 audio moves at its own pace and rewards trained ears.
- **Rushing Paper 2 reading** — missing the specific detail a question targets.
- **A scripted, unresponsive Individual Oral** — reciting instead of interacting.
- **Thin theme vocabulary** — hesitating because the active word bank is too small.

## A weekly LEARN → PRACTISE → GET-MARKED study system
Turn revision into a repeatable loop rather than passive rereading:

1. **LEARN** — take one prescribed theme and lock in its vocabulary and a few reusable structures. Use the free [French B SL course](/ib/courses/french-b-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/french-b-hl) to work theme-by-theme.
2. **PRACTISE** — do one timed task: a Paper 1 text type, a Paper 2 listening or reading set, or an Individual Oral run-through. Plan first, then perform to time.
3. **GET-MARKED** — mark it against the band descriptors, then [get an answer marked](/mark?subject=ib-french-b-hl) for a second opinion aligned to the criteria. Keep a log of your top three recurring errors and drill those first next week.

Rotate the loop across all five themes, weighting the ones where your band marking is weakest. Because French B is **exposure-hard rather than content-hard**, little-and-often wins — steady contact with the language beats a pre-exam sprint every time.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a 7 harder at HL than SL?
The core skills are the same, but HL adds the two literary works, a literature-based Individual Oral, longer and more sophisticated writing, and more demanding Paper 2 texts. HL 7s need that extra range and the literary component on top of clear top-band communication.

### How much does vocabulary matter?
A lot — but *active* vocabulary you can produce, not passive lists. Build theme-based phrase banks you can deploy in writing and speech, and refresh them little-and-often so recall is automatic under pressure.

### What is the biggest Paper 1 mistake?
Getting the text type or register wrong. Learn the conventions of each common text type so a blog reads like a blog and a formal email reads formal — then keep the message clear throughout.

### How is the Individual Oral different at SL and HL?
At SL it is based on a visual stimulus (an image linked to a theme); at HL it is based on an extract from one of the two literary works you have studied. Both reward fluent, developed, responsive speaking rather than recitation.

### How do I improve listening for Paper 2?
Regular exposure — listen to French audio at natural speed often, not just before the exam. Practise past listening sets under real conditions so audio pressure feels familiar, and train yourself to catch specific detail rather than gist.

## Bottom line
A 7 in IB French B is predictable, not lucky. Prioritise **clear communication first**, then build controlled range and accuracy on top. In Paper 1, nail the text type and register; in Paper 2, train careful reading and disciplined listening; in the Individual Oral, speak fluently and interact naturally. HL candidates add the literary works and longer writing. Cycle LEARN → PRACTISE → GET-MARKED every week across all five themes, drill your recurring errors, and keep steady contact with the language — that is how top candidates turn a 7 from a hope into a habit.
