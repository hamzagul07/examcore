## What are grade boundaries?

In simple terms, a grade boundary (or 'grade threshold' in official Cambridge terminology) is the minimum raw mark you need to achieve on a paper, or for the overall qualification, to be awarded a particular grade (A*, A, B, C, etc.). These are not fixed percentages; they are specific mark totals determined for each component and for the combined A-Level. Because the difficulty of exam papers can vary slightly between sessions, the boundaries are adjusted to compensate, ensuring fairness for all students.

## How Cambridge sets grade boundaries

The process of setting boundaries happens after all exam scripts have been marked. A panel of senior examiners and assessment experts analyses the performance of students across the globe. They review statistical data and samples of student work to determine the difficulty of the examination compared to previous years. Their goal is to ensure that a student who achieves a grade A in 2026 has demonstrated the same level of understanding as a student who achieved a grade A in 2025, regardless of whether the paper itself was slightly easier or harder.

Each paper contributes a specific number of raw marks to your final total. ## Frequently asked questions

### Can I use the 2025 boundaries to predict my 2026 grade?

Yes, using the 2025 boundaries is the most effective way to estimate your performance. However, treat them as a guide, not a guarantee. If the 2026 papers prove to be more challenging, the boundaries will likely be lower, and vice versa. They provide a benchmark for the level of performance required.

### What is the difference between a raw mark and a PUM?

Cambridge A-Levels are graded using raw marks. Your final grade is determined by comparing your total raw mark against the published threshold. Cambridge does not use a Uniform Mark Scale (UMS). The Percentage Uniform Mark (PUM) on your certificate indicates your performance relative to the grade boundaries but is not used to calculate the grade itself.

### Why are the boundaries for Paper 3/5 different from Paper 1/2/4?

Each paper has a different total mark, tests different skills, and has a unique level of difficulty. Paper 1 is multiple choice, Papers 2 and 4 are structured theory, while Papers 3 and 5 assess practical skills. Cambridge sets separate boundaries for each component to accurately reflect the performance standard required for that specific assessment.
