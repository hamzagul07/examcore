IB English A: Literature Higher Level is entirely skills-based: you analyse unseen extracts, compare studied works, write a 1,200–1,500 word **HL Essay**, and deliver an **Individual Oral**. Past papers train the formats; **markbands** tell you what examiners reward.

## Components at HL
**Paper 1** is a guided literary analysis of an unseen extract — comment on authorial choices, not plot. **Paper 2** is a comparative essay on two works you have studied. The **HL Essay** is an independent line of inquiry on one work. The **Individual Oral** links a global issue to one work and one non-literary text. Each has separate criteria — revise them explicitly.

## Analytical writing, not summary
Top-band scripts use **literary terminology** (imagery, narrative voice, structure, tone) and explain **effect on the reader**. Every paragraph should answer *how* and *why*, not only *what happens*. Compare this to our model responses in the [free Literature HL course](/ib/courses/english-a-literature-hl).

## A practical revision loop
1. Revise a literary topic (e.g. Paper 1 skills, comparative structure).
2. Write under timed conditions.
3. Self-mark with the official descriptors.
4. Upload for [criterion feedback](/mark?subject=ib-english-a-literature-hl).

## Frequently asked questions

### How many works should I know for Paper 2?
At least two per area of study you might face — with quotations, structural notes, and comparative angles prepared. Breadth beats memorising one work in extreme depth.

### What makes a strong HL Essay line of inquiry?
A narrow, arguable question about authorial choices — not a biography topic. Your essay should sustain an argument across 1,200–1,500 words with integrated quotations.

### How is the Individual Oral different from Paper 1?
The IO links a **global issue** to extracts from a literary and a non-literary body of work, with follow-up questions. Practise transitions between your two extracts.

## Bottom line
Literature HL is won by consistent analytical writing under time pressure. Know your works deeply, but practise placing every sentence in the top markband — that is what examiners score.
