Take **HL** if you find the impact of technology on society genuinely interesting and want more depth, breadth, and the added **HL extension** on challenges and interventions — and if you can commit the extra hours. Take **SL** if Digital Society is there for balance in your diploma rather than a subject you want to push furthest. The honest answer is: it depends on your interest and how the subject fits your wider HL package. Because Digital Society is a **new subject** (first assessment 2024, replacing ITGS), confirm the exact level differences against the current IB subject guide and your teacher — details are still bedding in. Below is what actually differs, how much harder HL really is, and a framework for deciding.

## What's the same at SL and HL
Both levels study the same thing: the impact of **digital systems and technologies on people and societies**, through the inquiry framework often called the **CCC model — concepts, content, and contexts**. Concepts include ideas such as **change, expression, identity, power, space, systems,** and **values and ethics**; contexts include the **cultural, economic, environmental, health, human-knowledge, political,** and **social**. SL and HL students work within this shared framework.

Both levels also sit **Paper 1** and **Paper 2** — source- and stimulus-based components that ask you to apply concepts to real examples — and both complete the internal **Inquiry Project**, a student-led investigation into a real digital-society example presented as a multimodal or written report. The core skill is identical at either level: apply concepts to **specific real examples**, analyse impacts on stakeholders, and evaluate interventions and ethics. Preview it in the free [Digital Society SL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-sl) and [HL course](/ib/courses/digital-society-hl).

## What HL adds
HL is not a different subject — it is the same framework studied with greater breadth and depth, **plus the HL extension**. The extension is an additional component focused on the **challenges and interventions** of digital society, based on a **pre-released stimulus** you prepare in advance. It rewards deeper synthesis: not just analysing an impact, but evaluating how societies might respond to a real-world challenge, and the limits of those responses.

The practical effect is more material to connect, a further component to prepare for, and higher expectations of evaluation. Here is the shape of the difference — treat the specifics as indicative and verify current details in the subject guide:

|  | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Framework (CCC) | Concepts, content, contexts | Same framework |
| Paper 1 | Yes (source/stimulus-based) | Yes (source/stimulus-based) |
| Paper 2 | Yes (source/stimulus-based) | Yes (source/stimulus-based) |
| HL extension | — | **Yes** — challenges & interventions, pre-released stimulus |
| Breadth & depth | Core treatment | Greater breadth and depth |
| Recommended teaching hours | Fewer (SL) | More (HL) — check current guide |
| Inquiry Project (IA) | Student-led investigation | **Same** student-led investigation |

The extra HL hours are not padding — they are the added extension component plus deeper, broader engagement with the shared framework, examined with more demanding synthesis and evaluation.

## How much harder is HL Digital Society?
The step up is mainly about the **HL extension** and the level of **evaluation** expected, rather than a harder kind of content. The thinking style — apply a concept to a real example, weigh stakeholders, evaluate interventions — is the same at both levels; HL simply asks for more of it, on a wider range of examples, with the additional pre-released-stimulus component to prepare. If you enjoy the subject and read widely about technology and society, HL is very doable. If Digital Society is a subject you tolerate rather than like, the extra breadth and the extension can feel heavy, and that effort may be better spent elsewhere in your diploma. For the technique side at either level, see [how to get a 7 in IB Digital Society](/blog/ib-digital-society-how-to-get-a-7).

## University fit
Digital Society is newer and less commonly named in specific entry requirements than long-established subjects, so be careful here. As a **Group 3 (individuals and societies)** subject, it can contribute to humanities, social-science, media, communications, and some technology-and-society pathways — but few courses will require it by name.

The critical rule: **check the exact entry requirements** for the specific courses and countries you are considering, and confirm how a given university treats Digital Society, since it is a recent addition. Do not assume it counts the same as an older Group 3 subject for a particular offer. If a degree needs a specific subject at HL (say, a named science or maths), make sure Digital Society is a complement to that plan, not a substitute for it. Read official course pages rather than relying on general advice.

## Who should take HL vs SL

**Take HL Digital Society if you:**

- Are genuinely interested in how technology shapes people and societies
- Enjoy building and evaluating arguments with real-world examples
- Can commit consistent time to extra breadth and the HL extension
- Want the deeper engagement HL offers and it fits your three-HL package

**Take SL Digital Society if you:**

- Want the subject for balance and breadth, not as a focus
- Are directing your HL energy into other subjects central to your plans
- Prefer a solid grounding without the added extension component
- Have confirmed your target courses are happy with SL (or don't require the subject by name)

## How to decide
1. **Start from your wider plan, not just the subject.** List target courses and read their published IB requirements; see where a Group 3 subject fits.
2. **Be honest about interest.** HL rewards students who find technology-and-society genuinely engaging; it drags for reluctant ones.
3. **Weigh your whole HL package.** You take three HLs — make sure Digital Society HL earns its place against your other priorities.
4. **Confirm how universities treat it.** Because it is new, don't assume; check official pages, especially if it is meant to satisfy a specific requirement.
5. **Preview real content.** Skim the SL and HL courses and try a few source-based questions before locking in.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the main difference between SL and HL?
Both sit Paper 1, Paper 2, and the Inquiry Project on the same concepts-content-contexts framework. **HL adds the HL extension** — a component on challenges and interventions built around a pre-released stimulus — plus greater breadth, depth, and evaluation. Confirm exact details in the current subject guide.

### Is HL Digital Society hard?
It is demanding mainly because of the added breadth and the extension component, and the higher expectation of evaluation — not because of a harder type of content. With genuine interest and steady practice on real examples, it is very achievable.

### Do universities accept Digital Society?
As a Group 3 subject it can contribute to many humanities and social-science pathways, but it is newer and rarely required by name. Because it is a recent addition, check how each specific university and course treats it rather than assuming.

### Is the Inquiry Project different at HL?
Both levels complete the same student-led Inquiry Project, marked against the same criteria. The taught breadth and the HL extension are where the level difference mainly lives — but verify against the current subject guide.

### Should I take it if I want a technology or computing degree?
It can complement such plans by covering the *societal* side of technology, but it is not a computer-science course. If a degree requires specific subjects (maths, computer science, a science), make sure Digital Society sits alongside them rather than replacing them. Check official requirements.

## Bottom line
IB Digital Society is the **same framework, the same Paper 1 and Paper 2, and the same Inquiry Project** at both levels — HL adds the **HL extension** on challenges and interventions, more breadth and depth, and higher expectations of evaluation. If you find technology-and-society genuinely interesting and can commit the extra hours, HL is rewarding; if the subject is for balance, SL gives a solid grounding.
