A 7 in IB Spanish B comes from communicating a clear message under exam conditions — the right text type in the right register, controlled language with useful range, and spoken answers that keep flowing. It is not won by memorising rare vocabulary or attempting risky grammar; the markbands reward the message first, then range and accuracy. This guide shows exactly how Paper 1, Paper 2 and the Individual Oral are marked, and where the marks that separate a 5 from a 7 actually sit.

## What a 7 actually takes
Grade boundaries shift slightly each session, so chasing an exact percentage is the wrong mindset. What matters is thinking in **markbands**. Every extended piece of writing and every spoken response is judged against level descriptors, and examiners place your work in the band it fits, then fine-tune within it. A single sophisticated sentence does not lift a muddled task into the top band; consistent top-band behaviour across the whole answer does.

Spanish B sits in **Group 2, language acquisition**, and the whole course is built around **five prescribed themes: identities; experiences; human ingenuity; social organisation; sharing the planet.** Top candidates move fluently between these themes because that is where the vocabulary, the writing prompts, and the oral stimuli all come from. Learning to read [IB markbands](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) like an examiner is the highest-leverage habit in the subject — the question is never "did I write enough?" but "which band does this sit in, and what one change would push it up a level?"

## The assessment and exactly how each part is marked
Spanish B is assessed by two external papers plus one internally-assessed spoken exam. Note the language-B funnel differs from many IB subjects: **there is no written IA — the internal component is the Individual Oral.**

| Component | Format | How it's marked | Where 7s are won |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| Paper 1 | Productive **writing** — choose one task, produce a text type | Markbands: message, language, and matching the text type/register | Clear message in the correct register with controlled range |
| Paper 2 | **Receptive skills** — listening + reading comprehension | Comprehension marks against the audio and texts | Accurate detail, inference, not missing answers under audio pressure |
| Individual Oral | Internally-assessed **spoken** exam | Markbands for message, language, and interactive skills | Keeping the conversation flowing with relevant, developed points |

**Paper 1** is productive writing. You **choose one task** and produce a **text type** — a blog, email, article, speech, proposal and so on — for a given **purpose, audience and register**. The examiner rewards a clear message that fits the task, appropriate register, and language range and accuracy. HL is longer and expects a wider range and register than SL, but the underlying skill is identical: pick the conventions of the text type and hold the right tone throughout.

**Paper 2** tests **receptive skills** — a **listening** comprehension and a **reading** comprehension. This is scored on comprehension: did you understand the specific detail, the gist, and the inferences the questions ask for? The pressure here is real-time listening (you cannot pause the audio in your head) and reading unfamiliar texts efficiently.

**The Individual Oral (IO)** is the internally-assessed spoken exam, and this is where SL and HL differ most. **At SL the IO is based on a visual stimulus** — an image linked to one of the themes. **At HL the IO is based on an extract from one of the two literary works** you study. Both are marked on message, language, and interactive skills. See the full breakdowns in the [Spanish B SL past papers guide](/blog/ib-spanish-b-sl-past-papers-guide) and the [HL past papers guide](/blog/ib-spanish-b-hl-past-papers-guide).

## Winning the Paper 1 writing band
This is the single biggest lever most students can pull quickly. Three things separate a top-band task from a middle-band one:

- **Correct text type and register.** A formal proposal reads nothing like a chatty blog post. Nail the conventions — greeting, sign-off, headings, tone, level of formality — that the task demands. Matching register is explicitly rewarded, and a strong task in the wrong register is capped below the top band however accurate the Spanish.
- **A clear, developed message.** Answer the actual prompt for the actual audience. Communication comes first in the markbands — a reader who understands you effortlessly scores you higher than a reader wading through ambitious errors.
- **Range and accuracy, in that order.** Show useful variety — tenses, connectives, some idiom — but **clarity beats risky complexity**. One controlled, correct sentence is worth more than a tangled one that half-works. Proofread accents and agreement; small accuracy slips add up.

A reliable Paper 1 routine: plan the text type and register before writing, cover every element of the prompt, then leave two minutes to check verbs, agreement and accents. This is exactly the skill our guide on [hitting the top essay markbands](/blog/ib-how-to-hit-the-top-markbands-in-essays) drills. Because the task defines the register and purpose, study [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained) so you respond to what is actually asked.

## Paper 2 receptive technique
Paper 2 rewards accurate comprehension, not fluent writing — so the technique is different. For **listening**, the biggest gains come from daily exposure: listen to Spanish audio *without transcripts* first, then check. Train yourself to catch numbers, dates, negatives, and the difference between what a speaker says and what they imply. For **reading**, skim for structure, then scan for the specific detail each question targets, and never leave a blank — an educated answer can score where silence cannot.

Core habits to drill until automatic:

- **Listening under pressure** — short clips daily, no pausing, then review what you missed and why.
- **Question mapping** — read the questions before/while you read the text so you know what to hunt for.
- **Detail vs inference** — some answers are stated outright; others require reading between the lines.
- **Watch negatives and qualifiers** — *no*, *nunca*, *casi*, *aunque* flip meanings and catch out rushed readers.

## The Individual Oral — keep it flowing
The IO rewards spontaneous, developed communication. At SL, describe and interpret the **visual stimulus**, link it to its theme, and be ready to discuss the wider issue it raises. At HL, work from your **literary extract** — show you understand it and can build a conversation around its ideas and the theme it connects to. In both cases the examiner is looking for a message that keeps developing and interactive skills that keep the exchange alive.

Practical drills: talk about each of the five themes out loud for two minutes, unscripted; prepare flexible vocabulary rather than memorised speeches (rote answers collapse the moment the examiner probes); and practise buying thinking time naturally (*bueno…*, *lo que quiero decir es…*) instead of freezing. **Active vocabulary** — words you can produce, not just recognise — is what carries the oral.

## Common mistakes that cap you at a 5
- **Wrong register or text type in Paper 1** — a formal task written casually, or vice versa.
- **Risky grammar over clear communication** — ambition that produces errors and obscures the message.
- **Only listening with transcripts** — you never build real-time comprehension for Paper 2.
- **Leaving Paper 2 answers blank** — no chance of a mark where a reasoned guess might score.
- **Memorised oral speeches** — they fall apart under follow-up questions; prepare flexible language instead.
- **Passive vocabulary only** — you recognise words but cannot produce them when writing or speaking.
- **Neglecting accents and agreement** — small, repeated slips that erode the accuracy band.

## A weekly LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED study system
Turn revision into a repeatable loop rather than passive rereading:

1. **LEARN** — take one theme and build active vocabulary and a couple of text-type templates around it. Use the free [Spanish B SL course](/ib/courses/spanish-b-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/spanish-b-hl) to work theme by theme.
2. **PRACTICE** — do a timed task: a Paper 1 text type, a Paper 2 listening/reading section, or two minutes of unscripted speaking on the theme. Then do it again from memory.
3. **GET-MARKED** — mark it against the band descriptors, then [get an answer marked](/mark?subject=ib-spanish-b-hl) for a second opinion aligned to the criteria. Keep a log of your top three recurring errors and drill those first next week.

Run this cycle across all five themes, weighting the ones where your band marking is weakest, and building listening exposure in daily.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a 7 harder at HL than SL?
The core writing and receptive skills are the same, but HL adds two literary works, a literature-based Individual Oral, longer writing, and more demanding Paper 2 texts. An HL 7 needs that extra range on top of clear, controlled communication.

### How much does vocabulary matter?
A lot — but **active** vocabulary you can produce, not obscure words you merely recognise. Build usable vocabulary around each of the five themes so you can write and speak fluently rather than reaching for a dictionary you cannot use in the exam.

### Should I aim for complex grammar?
No. The markbands reward the message first, then range and accuracy. Clear, controlled Spanish beats ambitious sentences riddled with errors. Show range where you can control it, and never at the cost of communication.

### How do I improve listening for Paper 2?
Daily exposure without transcripts, then checking. Train your ear on numbers, negatives and inference, and treat every missed answer as a specific thing to relearn. Real-time comprehension only comes from regular practice, not cramming.

### How is the Individual Oral marked?
On message, language, and interactive skills. At SL it starts from a visual stimulus; at HL from a literary extract. Keeping the conversation developing with relevant, flexible language matters more than delivering a polished, memorised speech. See [IB Spanish B SL vs HL](/blog/ib-spanish-b-sl-vs-hl).

## Bottom line
A 7 in IB Spanish B is predictable, not lucky. Learn active vocabulary theme by theme, practise real tasks to time, and mark honestly against the bands — prioritising a clear message in the right register over risky complexity. HL students add the literary works and the literature-based oral. Build listening exposure daily, cycle LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED every week, and drill your recurring errors. That is how top candidates turn a 7 from a hope into a habit.
