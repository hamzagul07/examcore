The strongest IB History Extended Essay topic is **narrow, at least ten years in the past, and answerable with primary and secondary sources you can weigh against each other** — driven by an analytical question, not a story. The History EE is a **4,000-word** independent investigation graded against five criteria (A–E), so the question you choose decides most of your mark before you write a word. This post shows what separates a workable RQ from an unmarkable one, gives an example research-questions table, and flags the traps examiners see every year. For the full criteria walk-through, see the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## History EE vs History IA — what's different
Both are source-based historical investigations, but the scale and demands differ sharply:

- **Length and depth.** The IA (historical investigation) is a short, tightly framed study; the History EE runs to **4,000 words** and expects a sustained, multi-strand argument.
- **Historiography.** The EE rewards genuine engagement with **historians' interpretations** — weighing how and why scholars disagree — not just source evaluation of individual documents.
- **Independence.** The EE is your own research project with a subject-qualified supervisor guiding rather than directing you.
- **Reflection.** The EE has its own reflection process recorded on the RPPF, marked under criterion E.

They cannot cover the same material, so keep your EE clearly distinct from your IA. For IA-specific structure, see the [IB History IA guide](/blog/ib-history-ia-guide).

## The two rules that rule out weak topics
Before anything else, test your idea against the two constraints that sink the most History EEs:

1. **The ten-year rule.** History EE topics must sit **at least ten years in the past**. Very recent events lack the historical distance and secondary literature the essay needs — a 2022 election is journalism, not history.
2. **It must be historical, not descriptive.** "The causes of the Cold War" is a textbook chapter. An EE needs a **debatable, evaluative** question — one where informed historians genuinely disagree.

## Choosing and narrowing your research question
Start from a period or theme you already enjoy, then narrow relentlessly: pick **one event, decision, policy, or figure**, choose the **historical debate** (cause, significance, extent, change), and confirm the **sources and historiography exist** before you commit. If you can't find historians arguing about it, the topic is too obscure or too settled — change the question, not the standards.

Proven question shapes:

- **"To what extent was [factor] responsible for [outcome]?"**
- **"Why did [event] happen when it did, rather than earlier or later?"**
- **"How far did [interpretation] hold, in the case of [specific example]?"**
- **"How significant was [individual/policy] to [outcome]?"**

## Example research questions: too broad ❌ → sharpened ✅
Treat these as **structural models**, not topics to copy — your question and sources must be your own, and always clear the idea with your supervisor.

| Theme | Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
|---|---|---|
| Conflict | What caused World War One? | To what extent was the alliance system responsible for the escalation of the July Crisis in 1914? |
| Leadership | Was Stalin a good leader? | How significant was the 1936–38 purge of the Red Army to Soviet military failures of 1941? |
| Decolonisation | The end of the British Empire | Why did Britain withdraw from Palestine in 1948 rather than negotiate a longer transition? |
| Social change | The US civil rights movement | To what extent did the 1955–56 Montgomery bus boycott change segregation policy in Alabama? |
| Economic history | The Great Depression | How far did New Deal relief programmes reduce unemployment in one US state between 1933 and 1937? |

Notice the pattern: each ✅ version fixes a **place, a date range, and a debate** — turning an unmarkable survey into a 4,000-word argument.

## Methodology and pitfalls
A History EE is built from **evidence and historiography**, not opinion. Aim for a genuine mix of **primary sources** (documents, speeches, statistics, records) and **secondary sources** (scholarly histories), and make the essay's spine an argument that **weighs competing interpretations** rather than narrating events. Evaluate key sources for origin, purpose, value and limitations where it advances your case, and footnote as you draft so citation never becomes a last-minute scramble.

Common pitfalls:

- **Narrative instead of argument** — retelling the story rather than answering the question.
- **Too broad a question** — no place, date range, or debate, so nothing gets analysed in depth.
- **Recent events** — breaching the ten-year rule and starving the essay of secondary literature.
- **No historiography** — evidence with no engagement in how historians have interpreted it.
- **Weak or one-sided sourcing** — relying on a single account, or on encyclopaedic websites.
- **Neglecting reflection** — a thin RPPF, which examiners mark directly under criterion E.

## How marking works
The EE is externally marked out of roughly **34 marks** and graded **A–E**, across five criteria: **A – Focus and method**, **B – Knowledge and understanding**, **C – Critical thinking**, **D – Presentation**, and **E – Engagement** (your reflections on the RPPF). Combined with Theory of Knowledge, a strong EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus points** to your diploma. For History, criterion C carries a lot: it rewards the analysis, evaluation and historiographical judgement that a narrative essay simply cannot show. ## Frequently asked questions

### How recent can my History EE topic be?
Your topic should be **at least ten years in the past**. Very recent events lack the historical distance and scholarly interpretation the essay depends on — confirm the current rule with your supervisor and the *History guide*.

### Do I need primary sources?
You need a genuine range of **primary and secondary sources** and, crucially, the ability to weigh **historians' interpretations** against each other. A strong secondary historiographical debate is as important as any single primary document.

### How long is the History EE?
Up to **4,000 words**. Going over is penalised, so a narrow question you can answer fully — with room for historiography — is a real advantage.

### Does my EE subject have to match an HL subject?
It's recommended but not required. You do need a **subject-qualified supervisor** in History to guide the project and sign off the reflections.

### How is the History EE graded?
Externally, out of about **34 marks** across five criteria (A–E), converted to a letter grade **A–E**. With TOK it can add **up to 3 diploma points**.

## Bottom line
A strong History EE starts from a **narrow, debatable question set at least ten years in the past**, backed by real primary and secondary sources and genuine **historiography**. Fix a place, a date range and a debate, then argue rather than narrate.
