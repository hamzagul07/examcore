The strongest IB English A Extended Essay topic takes **literary text(s) with real substance and asks a focused question about how language, form or context creates meaning** — never plot summary or biography. The English EE is a **4,000-word** literary investigation graded against five criteria (A–E), so your text choice and question decide most of your mark before you write a word. This post shows what makes an analytical RQ, gives an example research-questions table, and flags the traps examiners see every year. For the full criteria walk-through, see the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## English EE vs English A IA — what's different
The English A IA is the **Individual Oral**, a spoken analysis of how a global issue appears in your works. The EE is a very different task:

- **Written and extended.** The EE is a **4,000-word** written literary essay, not a ten-minute oral.
- **Self-chosen text(s).** You choose your literary text(s) and a question, rather than working from set extracts.
- **Sustained argument.** It rewards a single, tightly focused line of literary analysis developed in depth across the whole essay.
- **Its own reflection.** The EE has a separate reflection process recorded on the RPPF, marked under criterion E.

The two cannot cover the same material, so keep your EE distinct from your IO. For the oral, see the [IB English A IA guide](/blog/ib-english-a-ia-guide).

## English A EE is literary — clarify your category
An English **A** Extended Essay is a **literary** essay: it analyses **literary text(s)** and how meaning is made, not language use in general and not an author's life. Within Language A, the essay falls into one of three literary **categories** — broadly one text, a comparison of texts, or text(s) in relation to context and reception — so **confirm the exact category and its requirements with your supervisor** before you commit. Draw on whichever route you study: [English A: Literature](/ib/courses/english-a-literature-hl) or [English A: Language & Literature](/ib/courses/english-a-lang-lit-hl).

## Choosing and narrowing your research question
Start from **text(s) you find rich** — dense enough in technique to sustain 4,000 words — then narrow to a single analytical thread. Pick a **theme, technique, or effect**, decide what you're actually arguing about it, and check the text genuinely supports that reading before you commit. A good test: if your question could be answered by summarising the plot, it isn't a literary EE question yet.

Proven question shapes:

- **"How does [writer] use [technique] to present [theme/effect] in [text]?"**
- **"To what extent does [formal choice] shape [meaning] in [text]?"**
- **"How do [two texts] use [technique] to explore [theme]?"**
- **"What is the effect of [structural/narrative choice] on [the reader's understanding of X]?"**

## Example research questions: too broad ❌ → sharpened ✅
Treat these as **structural models**, not topics to copy — your question and text(s) must be your own, and always clear the idea and category with your supervisor.

| Focus | Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
|---|---|---|
| Single text | What is *Beloved* about? | How does Toni Morrison use fragmented narrative structure to present the persistence of trauma in *Beloved*? |
| Character | Is Hamlet a hero? | To what extent does Shakespeare's use of soliloquy shape the reader's judgement of Hamlet's delay? |
| Imagery | Nature in poetry | How does imagery of light and darkness construct despair across three of Sylvia Plath's *Ariel* poems? |
| Comparison | Two novels about war | How do Vonnegut and O'Brien use unreliable narration to question the possibility of truthful war writing? |
| Form | Symbolism in a play | What is the effect of the recurring seagull motif on the presentation of thwarted ambition in Chekhov's *The Seagull*? |

Notice the pattern: each ✅ version names a **specific technique**, a **specific effect**, and a **defined text or set of texts** — turning a book report into a 4,000-word argument.

## Methodology and pitfalls
A literary EE is built from **close analysis of the text**, not summary or opinion. Anchor every claim in specific evidence — quotations, structural features, formal choices — and always link that evidence back to **how** the writing creates the meaning you're arguing for. Where context or a critical reading strengthens the analysis, use it in service of your argument rather than as decoration. Cite quotations and any secondary criticism consistently as you draft.

Common pitfalls:

- **Plot summary** — retelling the story instead of analysing technique.
- **Biography** — writing about the author's life rather than the text's craft.
- **Too broad a question** — no named technique or effect, so nothing gets analysed in depth.
- **Thin text choice** — a text without enough substance to fill 4,000 words of analysis.
- **Unbalanced comparison** — one text covered far more than the other.
- **Neglecting reflection** — a thin RPPF, which examiners mark directly under criterion E.

## How marking works
The EE is externally marked out of roughly **34 marks** and graded **A–E**, across five criteria: **A – Focus and method**, **B – Knowledge and understanding**, **C – Critical thinking**, **D – Presentation**, and **E – Engagement** (your reflections on the RPPF). Combined with Theory of Knowledge, a strong EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus points** to your diploma. For English, criterion C carries a lot: it rewards the sustained close analysis and evaluation of literary effect that plot summary simply cannot show. ## Frequently asked questions

### Can my English A EE be about language rather than literature?
An English **A** Extended Essay is **literary** — it analyses literary text(s) and how meaning is made. Confirm the exact **category** and its requirements with your supervisor before you commit.

### How many texts should I use?
It depends on your category — a single-text study or a comparison are both valid routes. Choose **text(s) with enough substance** to sustain 4,000 words of close analysis, and keep any comparison balanced.

### How long is the English EE?
Up to **4,000 words**. Going over is penalised, so a focused question on a well-chosen text is a real advantage over an over-ambitious survey.

### Does my EE subject have to match an HL subject?
It's recommended but not required. You do need a **subject-qualified supervisor** in English A to guide the project and sign off the reflections.

### How is the English EE graded?
Externally, out of about **34 marks** across five criteria (A–E), converted to a letter grade **A–E**. With TOK it can add **up to 3 diploma points**.

## Bottom line
A strong English A EE pairs a **substantial literary text with a focused question about technique, form or effect** — never plot summary or biography. Confirm your literary **category** with your supervisor, name the technique and the effect you're analysing, and argue in depth rather than describe.
