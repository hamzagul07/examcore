The long wait between finishing your Cambridge exams and results day can be more stressful than the exams themselves. With results for the June 2026 series released on **13 August 2026**, the key is to use this time constructively, focusing on what you can control while letting go of what you can't. This period is an opportunity for rest, personal growth, and strategic planning.

## Acknowledge the Wait (and Let It Go)

Once your final exam script is submitted, the outcome is out of your hands. It is completely normal to feel anxious, but worrying will not change your marks. Acknowledging this simple fact is the first step to making this waiting period productive rather than painful. Try to consciously redirect your thoughts when you find yourself dwelling on 'what ifs'; the work is done, and now you must trust the process.

## Rest, Recover, and Recharge

You have just completed a marathon of revision and exams, so post-exam burnout is a real phenomenon. Give yourself permission to take a proper break, free from academic pressure. This means more than just not studying; it means actively recharging by sleeping well, reconnecting with friends and family, and dedicating time to hobbies you enjoy. Whether it's binge-watching a series, getting outdoors, or picking up a novel, this downtime is essential for your wellbeing and will ensure you're ready for the next chapter, whatever it may be.

## Get a Head Start (Without the Pressure)

If you're feeling rested and a bit restless, you can use this time to gently prepare for the next academic year. This isn't about intense study; it's about building confidence and familiarity. You could look over the syllabus for your A-Level or IB subjects, read the first chapter of a textbook, or watch some introductory videos on the first few topics. For a practical exercise, you could even try to [mark a past paper](/mark) for a new subject to understand its structure, all in a low-stakes environment.

## Build a Life Skill

The long summer break is a golden opportunity to develop skills that will benefit you long after results day. Consider learning to drive, taking a first aid course, or volunteering for a local charity. Getting a part-time job provides not only work experience and financial independence but also a valuable distraction. You could even start learning a new language with an app or online course. These activities add depth to your CV and university applications, and they build confidence outside of an academic context.

## Plan for Every Outcome

A great way to manage anxiety is to have a clear plan. Instead of fixating on one desired outcome, prepare for three broad scenarios: results that are better than expected, results that are on target, and results that are below your expectations. For the 'better' scenario, what new university or course options might open up? For the 'below' scenario, research the process for an **Enquiry About Results (EAR)**, or 'remark', and look up the deadlines for the Cambridge November resit series. Having a clear, step-by-step plan for each possibility means you can react calmly and logically on results day, and our [Cambridge results day guide](/blog/cambridge-results-day-august-2026-guide) walks you through every option.

## Estimate Your Grade (with a Big Pinch of Salt)

If you know your raw marks from mock exams or self-marked papers, it’s tempting to try and predict your grade. You can use our [grade boundary calculator](/tools/grade-boundary-calculator) to see how your raw mark might have translated to a grade in previous years. However, you must treat this as a very rough estimate. **Grade boundaries** are set each year after marking is complete to ensure fairness, and they can and do change. Using old boundaries can be a useful guide, but don't let it create false hope or unnecessary panic; the only numbers that matter are the ones released on 13 August.

## Frequently asked questions

### When are the June 2026 Cambridge International results released?

Cambridge International will release results to schools on 12 August 2026. You will be able to access your results directly from Cambridge or through your school on **Thursday, 13 August 2026**. Check with your school's exams officer for the exact time and method they will use to distribute them.

### What should I do if my results are not what I expected?

First, don't panic. Speak with your teachers and your school's exams officer immediately. They can offer advice on all your options, which may include an Enquiry About Results (a remark) or retaking an exam in the November series. Our comprehensive [results day guide](/blog/cambridge-results-day-august-2026-guide) details these processes.

### Is it worth trying to guess my grade using old grade boundaries?

While it can provide a rough indication, it's not a reliable prediction. Grade boundaries change every series based on the overall performance of the cohort and the difficulty of the papers. Relying too heavily on past data can lead to disappointment or false security. Use it as a guide, not a guarantee.

## Bottom line

The wait for your Cambridge results is a test of patience, not a continuation of your exams. Use this unique period to rest, develop new skills, and make practical plans for all possible outcomes. By focusing on what you can control, you can turn this anxious wait into a valuable and productive time.
