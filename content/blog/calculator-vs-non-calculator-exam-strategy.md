Success in your Cambridge exams depends on tailoring your strategy to whether a calculator is permitted. For calculator papers, the focus is on speed, accuracy, and efficient use of your device. For non-calculator papers, the emphasis shifts to robust mental and written methods, estimation, and clearly demonstrating your thought process to earn **method marks**.

## Know the Battlefield: Is a Calculator Allowed?
The first step in your preparation is to know the rules of engagement for each specific exam paper. Calculator policies vary not just between subjects (like Physics and Mathematics) but also between different papers within the same subject. The definitive source of information is the front cover of the question paper itself. Always check the instructions on recent past papers during your revision and, most importantly, read the front of your actual exam paper on the day. Never make assumptions; a rule that applied to Paper 1 might not apply to Paper 2.

## Strategy for Calculator Papers: Speed and Precision
On a calculator paper, your calculator is your most important tool, and you must be a master of it. This isn't just about knowing how to add and subtract; it's about using it quickly and accurately under pressure. Before the exam, practise using functions like memory (M+), brackets for complex fractions, and ensuring you are in the correct mode (**degrees** vs. **radians** is a classic pitfall in maths). Wasting time fumbling with your calculator or making a slip can cost you valuable marks and momentum. ## Strategy for Non-Calculator Papers: Method and Clarity
When a calculator is forbidden, the examiners are testing a different skill set: your fundamental mathematical fluency and your ability to reason logically. Your preparation should focus on drilling mental arithmetic, times tables, and standard written algorithms for long multiplication, division, and fractions. Since you can't just type in a calculation, showing your working becomes absolutely essential. Many marks are awarded for the correct method, even if a small arithmetic error leads to the wrong final answer. Write out every step logically and clearly, as if you are explaining your solution to someone else.

## A Calculator is a Tool, Not a Brain
A common mistake is to believe a calculator is a substitute for understanding the underlying concepts. It is not. In subjects like Physics, Chemistry, and Mathematics, a significant portion of the marks are for selecting the right formula, rearranging it correctly, and substituting the correct values from the question. A calculator can only perform the final arithmetic step; it cannot analyse the question, identify the required method, or interpret the result in context. Your brain does all the heavy lifting, and that is what earns the majority of the marks.

## Use Mark Schemes to Decode the Exam
The single best way to understand what examiners want is to use official mark schemes during your revision. When you [mark a past paper](/mark), pay close attention to how marks are allocated. You will see 'M' marks for method, 'A' marks for accuracy, and 'B' marks for independent points. This reveals that even in a calculator paper, setting up the problem correctly earns you most of the credit. ## Frequently asked questions

### Which calculator model is best for Cambridge exams?
There is no single 'best' model, but you must use one permitted by Cambridge International regulations (check the official syllabus handbook). The most important factor is familiarity. Choose a standard scientific calculator and learn its functions inside out long before the exam. Don't buy a new, unfamiliar model just before your papers.

### What happens if my calculator runs out of battery in the exam?
This is a preventable disaster. Always check your calculator and consider putting in fresh batteries a week before your exams begin. If the worst happens, inform an invigilator immediately. They cannot help you with the questions, but they may have a basic replacement calculator you can borrow. Relying on this is a huge risk.

### How can I improve my speed in non-calculator papers?
Speed comes from practice and confidence. Regularly work through non-calculator questions under timed conditions. Drill your times tables and number bonds until they are automatic. Practise estimating answers before you begin a full calculation; this helps you spot obvious errors quickly and builds your number sense. Don't be afraid of written methods—they are often faster and more reliable than trying to do everything in your head.

## Bottom line
Ultimately, your exam strategy shouldn't be 'calculator' vs 'non-calculator', but 'prepared' vs 'unprepared'. Master the tools you are allowed, whether that's a physical device or your own mental arithmetic. A deep understanding of the subject content, combined with targeted practice using official mark schemes, is the universal key to success.
