The official Cambridge O-Level Mathematics (4024) grade boundaries for 2026 will not be decided until after all exam papers have been marked. Cambridge International sets these **grade thresholds** for each exam session to ensure standards are consistent year-on-year, releasing them on results day. For the June 2026 exam series, this will be on 13 August 2026.

## What are grade boundaries?
Grade boundaries, which Cambridge officially calls 'grade thresholds', are the minimum raw marks a student needs to achieve a specific grade. For 4024 Mathematics, thresholds are published for each of the two papers (the non-calculator and the calculator paper) and, most importantly, for the overall qualification. For example, there will be a minimum total mark for a grade A, a minimum for a grade B, and so on. These are absolute marks, not percentages, and they are the definitive measure used to award your final grade.

## Why do grade boundaries change each year?
Grade boundaries are not fixed because the difficulty of exam papers can vary slightly from one session to another. To be fair to all students, Cambridge adjusts the boundaries to compensate for these minor differences. If a paper is found to be slightly more challenging than the previous year's, the grade boundaries may be lowered. Conversely, if a paper is more straightforward, the boundaries might be raised. This process, known as **comparable outcomes**, ensures that a student of a certain ability will receive the same grade regardless of the exam session they sit.

## How are 4024 Mathematics papers marked?
Your final grade in O-Level Maths is determined by your combined performance across two papers. Examiners award marks based on a detailed mark scheme, rewarding not just the final correct answer but also the correct application of mathematical methods. This is why showing your **working** clearly is absolutely critical; you can earn substantial method marks even if you make a calculation error. Each correct step or piece of reasoning brings you closer to your target grade, as every single raw mark counts towards the overall total that will be compared against the final grade boundaries.

## When will the 2026 grade boundaries be released?
Cambridge International will publish the official grade boundaries for the June 2026 examination series on results day, which is scheduled for **13 August 2026**. They will appear on the Cambridge public website at the same time your school or exam centre receives your results. ## How can I estimate my grade before results day?
The most effective way to estimate your potential grade is to use recent past papers. After you complete a paper under timed, exam-like conditions, you should mark it strictly using the official mark scheme. Once you have a raw score for each paper, you can compare your total mark against the grade boundaries from the last two or three exam sessions (e.g., June 2025, November 2025). This will give you a realistic, though not guaranteed, indication of the grade you are tracking towards. You can find all the necessary past papers and their corresponding grade boundaries on the MarkScheme page for [4024 O-Level Mathematics](/subjects/4024).

## Frequently asked questions

### Is it true that you need a certain percentage to get an A?
No, this is a common misconception. Grades are awarded based on the raw mark thresholds set for that specific session, not on pre-defined percentages. The mark required for an A can vary significantly year-on-year depending on the difficulty of the papers, making any fixed percentage target unreliable and potentially misleading.

### Where can I find past grade boundaries for 4024 Maths?
Cambridge International publishes official 'Grade Threshold' documents for every past exam session on its public website. For a more user-friendly experience, you can find a consolidated history of boundaries on MarkScheme, which makes it easier to compare different sessions at a glance and understand the typical mark ranges for each grade.

### If I do badly on one paper, can I still get a good grade?
Yes, absolutely. Your final grade is determined by your **overall** raw mark, which is the sum of your scores from both the non-calculator and calculator papers. A very strong performance on one paper can certainly help to compensate for a weaker result on the other, so it is vital to fight for every mark in both exams.

### How can I get a better idea of my current level?
The best method is to simulate an exam. Complete a full past paper under timed conditions, then use the official mark scheme to grade your work honestly. Using a tool to [mark a paper](/mark) can help you score accurately and identify areas for improvement. This process gives you a concrete raw score, which is the most valuable piece of data for tracking your progress.
