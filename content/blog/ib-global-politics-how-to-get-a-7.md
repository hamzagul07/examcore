A 7 in IB Global Politics comes from one repeated habit under exam pressure: building a clear argument, weighing it from more than one perspective, and grounding it in real contemporary examples while using the course's key concepts explicitly. Knowing the theory earns you a middling grade; description and one-sided answers cap you there. The top markband rewards evaluation, balance, and precise use of concepts like power, sovereignty, human rights, development, and peace and conflict. This guide shows how each assessment is marked and where the marks that separate a 5 from a 7 actually sit. (Exact timings, word counts, and weightings shift between sessions, so confirm the specifics in the current IB subject guide and with your teacher.)

## What a 7 actually takes

Grade boundaries move slightly each session, so do not fixate on a percentage. What matters is thinking in **markbands**. Every extended response and structured answer is placed against level descriptors: the examiner decides which band your answer fits, then fine-tunes within it. A single sharp insight does not lift a descriptive essay into the top band — consistent top-band behaviour across the whole answer does. Learning to read [IB markbands](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) like an examiner is the highest-leverage habit in the subject.

Global Politics is built on four core concepts studied through a "people, power and politics" lens, with a required focus on real-world political issues:

- **Power, sovereignty and international relations**
- **Human rights**
- **Development**
- **Peace and conflict**

Top candidates move fluently between these concepts, apply them to current events, and never treat them as background theory to be described. The best answers *use* a concept to advance an argument rather than defining it and moving on.

## The assessments and how each is marked

Global Politics is assessed by external papers plus an internal engagement project. At a stable level, the shape is:

| Assessment | Format | How it's marked | Where 7s are won |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paper 1 | Source/stimulus-based structured questions on a core topic | Markbands rewarding accurate reading, use of sources, and evaluation | Comparing and evaluating sources, not just describing them |
| Paper 2 | Extended-response essays across the core units | Markbands rewarding argument, balance, concepts, and examples | The evaluation and multi-perspective analysis |
| Engagement project (IA) | Analytical report linking a real political engagement to theory | Criterion-marked coursework | Linking lived experience to concepts and theory |
| HL extension (HL only) | Case studies of global political challenges, presented orally/as video | Assessed on the case studies | Depth of case analysis and concept use |

**Paper 1** is source-based. You read stimulus material on a core topic and answer structured questions that build from comprehension to evaluation. The marks that lift you are in the higher-order parts: comparing sources, weighing their reliability or perspective, and reaching a supported view — not paraphrasing what a source says.

**Paper 2** is where markband thinking matters most. These are extended essays across the four core units, and the top band explicitly rewards a clear argument, balanced multi-perspective evaluation, real contemporary examples, and explicit use of key concepts. Miss the balance or drop the examples and you are capped below the top band, however fluent your writing.

**The engagement project** is your internal assessment: you engage with a real political issue and write an analytical report connecting that experience to political theory and concepts. It is criterion-marked coursework and represents accessible marks too many students leave late. Check the exact task requirements and current criteria in your subject guide and with your teacher.

**The HL extension** adds a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies presented orally or as video. This is the core SL-to-HL difference — see [IB Global Politics SL vs HL](/blog/ib-global-politics-sl-vs-hl) for the full comparison.

## Winning the Paper 2 evaluation band

This is the single biggest differentiator between a 5 and a 7. Most students can *describe* a political issue; far fewer can *evaluate* it to the top band. Two things are close to non-negotiable.

- **Balanced, multi-perspective evaluation.** Weigh the issue from more than one viewpoint — different states, institutions, theoretical lenses (realist vs liberal vs critical), or stakeholders — then commit to a justified overall judgement. "There are many views" is not evaluation; "these views conflict, and given the evidence, X is more persuasive because…" is.
- **A reasoned, supported conclusion.** The strongest essays do not trail off. They land a verdict that answers the exact question, tied to the argument you built rather than tacked on.

A reliable Paper 2 structure: a thesis that directly answers the question, analysis paragraphs that each advance a distinct argument with a concept and a real example, at least one paragraph that genuinely engages the counter-perspective, and a conclusion that reaches a supported judgement. Because *command terms* set the task, misreading "evaluate" or "discuss" as "describe" is fatal — study [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained) so *examine*, *evaluate*, *discuss*, and *to what extent* each trigger the right response.

## Real examples and key concepts — the top-band signals

Two habits mark an answer as top-band. First, **real contemporary examples**: named, specific, and dated where possible, used to advance the argument rather than dropped in as decoration. One precise case you actually analyse beats three vague gestures at "some countries." Build a small bank of examples across the four core concepts — a sovereignty dispute, a human-rights case, a development debate, a conflict or peace process — that you can deploy and adapt.

Second, **explicit use of key concepts**. The markbands reward answers that weave power, sovereignty, human rights, development, and peace and conflict directly into the analysis. Do not just mention a concept — use it as a lens that sharpens the argument. An essay on an intervention that explicitly weighs sovereignty against human-rights protection is doing exactly what the top band asks for.

## Common mistakes that cap you at a 5

- **Describing instead of evaluating.** Strong description with no judgement stalls in the middle bands.
- **One-sided answers.** No counter-perspective means the evaluation band never opens.
- **Vague or missing examples.** Generic references block the top band; the markbands want specific, real cases.
- **Concepts named but not used.** Defining "sovereignty" and moving on is not the same as analysing through it.
- **Misreading the command term** — writing a description when the task says *evaluate* or *discuss*.
- **Paper 1: paraphrasing sources** instead of comparing and evaluating them.
- **Leaving the engagement project late**, sacrificing accessible criterion-marked marks.

## A weekly LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED system

Turn revision into a repeatable loop rather than passive rereading, cycling across the four core concepts:

1. **LEARN** — take one syllabus point from a core unit, study the theory, and lock a real example and the relevant concept to it. Use the free [Global Politics SL course](/ib/courses/global-politics-sl) or [HL course](/ib/courses/global-politics-hl) to work through the content unit by unit.
2. **PRACTICE** — do a timed question on that point: a Paper 1 source set or a Paper 2 essay. Plan the argument first, then write to time. Drill topics with the [SL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/global-politics-sl) and [HL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/global-politics-hl).
3. **GET-MARKED** — mark it against the band descriptors, then [get an answer marked](/mark) for a second read aligned to the criteria. Keep a mistake log of your top three recurring errors — usually "didn't evaluate" or "no real example" — and drill those first next week.

Run this across all four concepts, weighting the ones where your band marking is weakest, and start the engagement project early rather than treating it as an afterthought.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is a 7 harder at HL than SL?

The core essay and source skills are the same, but HL adds the HL extension — a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies. HL 7s need that extra depth on top of top-band evaluation across the four core concepts. See [IB Global Politics SL vs HL](/blog/ib-global-politics-sl-vs-hl).

### How important are real-world examples?

For Paper 2 evaluation, they are decisive — the top band rewards specific, real contemporary examples used to advance the argument. Build a small bank of precise, named cases across the four core concepts and practise deploying them.

### What separates a 5 from a 7?

Mostly evaluation and balance. A 5 tends to describe and argue one side; a 7 weighs multiple perspectives, uses concepts explicitly, and lands a reasoned judgement that answers the exact question. Getting the command term right is part of this — see [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained).

### How do I handle Paper 1 sources?

Move beyond paraphrasing. Compare sources against each other, consider their perspective and reliability, and use them to support an evaluated view. The higher-order parts of Paper 1 reward using sources, not summarising them.

### When should I start the engagement project?

Early. It is criterion-marked coursework — accessible marks — and rushing it late is a common way to lose grade. For general guidance see the [IB internal assessment guide](/blog/ib-internal-assessment-complete-guide), and confirm the current task and criteria with your teacher.

## Bottom line

A 7 in IB Global Politics is predictable, not lucky. Build a clear argument, evaluate it from more than one perspective, ground every point in a real contemporary example, and use the four core concepts explicitly — that combination is exactly what the top markband rewards, and description or one-sided answers are exactly what cap you at a 5. Match the command term, mark honestly against the bands, start the engagement project early, and cycle LEARN → PRACTICE → GET-MARKED every week.
