Media Studies revision clicks when you treat papers as **repeatable analytical moves** — media language, theory, industry context and micro-detail from set texts. This guide ranks high-frequency themes across Cambridge **9607** from **2019–2025**.

> **Key takeaway:** Marks come from **specific examples from the text** — generic "stereotype" paragraphs without evidence stay in low bands.

## How this list was built

We weighted analysis-question patterns, coursework mark-band language, and examiner comments on weak theory application.

## A-Level Media Studies (9607)

| Priority | Theme / skill | Why it repeats |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Media language (camera, edit, sound, layout)** | Close analysis on set products |
| 2 | **Representation** | Gender, ethnicity, class — theory + textual proof |
| 3 | **Audience & consumption** | Active/passive, demographics, fandom |
| 4 | **Media industries & ownership** | Conglomerates, vertical integration, regulation |
| 5 | **Genre & narrative** | Codes, conventions, hybridity |
| 6 | **Ideology & values** | Dominant vs alternative readings |
| 7 | **Global vs local media** | Cultural imperialism, niche streaming |
| 8 | **Evaluation & comparison** | Two texts, weighed judgement |

## Band traps

| Weak habit | Fix |
|------------|-----|
| Plot summary | **Micro-detail** — one frame, headline, or edit |
| Theory name-dropping | Apply **one theorist** to this text's evidence |
| No industry link | Tie representation to **who produces/distributes** |
| Coursework generalities | Same discipline — evidence in every paragraph |
