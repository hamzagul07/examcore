A strong IB Psychology Extended Essay is a **secondary-research, library-based essay** — you analyse and evaluate existing published studies and theories to answer a narrow research question, and you do **not** run your own experiment on human participants. The best topic is one where two or more established studies or explanations genuinely disagree, so you have something to argue rather than describe. A "good" psychology EE research question is (1) answerable using published, peer-reviewed research you can actually access, (2) narrow enough to argue in 4,000 words, and (3) evaluative — it forces you to weigh evidence, contrast approaches, or judge how well a theory holds up, rather than just summarising what psychologists have found. Get those three right and the critical-thinking marks, which decide most grades, become reachable. This post shows how to choose and sharpen a topic, with an example research-question table you can adapt.

## Psychology EE vs the Psychology IA

These are different pieces of work and students confuse them constantly. The **Internal Assessment** is a small experimental study you design and run yourself — one manipulated variable, one measured outcome, written up as a report. The **Extended Essay** is the opposite: a 4,000-word argument built entirely from *other people's* published research. You are not allowed to collect primary data from human participants for a psychology EE, because you cannot meet the ethical standards required for original research at this level. If your idea only works by surveying, interviewing, or experimenting on people, it belongs (in simplified form) in the IA, not the EE.

For the experimental side, see the [IB Psychology IA guide](/blog/ib-psychology-ia-guide). This post is only about the essay.

## What a psychology EE actually asks you to do

Because you are working from published sources, your job is analytical, not empirical. A top essay usually does one of these things:

- **Contrasts two explanations of the same behaviour** — for example a biological versus a cognitive account, and evaluates which the evidence supports better.
- **Tests how well a theory holds up** against studies that support and challenge it, ending with a reasoned judgement.
- **Evaluates the evidence for a specific claimed effect** — how robust is it, and under what conditions does it hold?
- **Examines a methodological or ethical issue** across a body of studies.

Notice that every one of these ends in a *judgement*. Descriptive essays — "an overview of memory" — cannot reach the top bands because there is nothing to evaluate.

## Choosing and narrowing a research question

Most weak psychology EEs fail at the question stage: the topic is far too broad, so the essay becomes a textbook summary. Narrowing means fixing the behaviour, the population or context, and the specific studies or theories you will pit against each other. The table below shows the move from a vague topic to an arguable question.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
| --- | --- |
| How does stress affect memory? | To what extent does acute stress impair the retrieval of episodic memory, according to cortisol-based studies? |
| Is eyewitness testimony reliable? | To what extent does the misinformation effect explain errors in adult eyewitness recall of a single event? |
| What causes depression? | To what extent do cognitive versus biological explanations account for the maintenance of major depressive disorder? |
| Does social media harm teenagers? | To what extent does the evidence support a link between social comparison on image-based platforms and body dissatisfaction in adolescent girls? |
| How does culture affect behaviour? | To what extent does individualism–collectivism explain cross-cultural differences in conformity, based on replications of Asch-type studies? |
| Is intelligence genetic? | How convincingly do twin-study findings support a heritable component of general intelligence? |

The sharpened versions all share a shape: a **command term** ("to what extent", "how convincingly"), a **specific behaviour or effect**, and an **implied body of evidence** to weigh. That shape is what makes an essay arguable. If you want to see how command terms drive marks across IB, read [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained).

## Method: how to build the essay from sources

Your "methodology" in a psychology EE is a research strategy, not a lab procedure. State clearly which explanations or studies you will use, why you selected them, and how you will evaluate them. Strong essays:

- Draw on **peer-reviewed studies and named theories**, cited properly, rather than textbooks and news articles.
- **Evaluate** each study — sample, design, ecological validity, replication, cultural bias — instead of taking findings at face value.
- **Contrast** approaches deliberately, so the argument moves forward rather than listing studies side by side.
- Reach a **reasoned, hedged conclusion** that answers the exact question asked.

## Pitfalls that cost marks

- **Trying to run your own study.** Primary data collection from participants is not permitted for the psychology EE. Design it as secondary research from the start.
- **A descriptive question.** "What is the multi-store model of memory?" has no argument. Add a command term and an evaluative angle.
- **Too broad.** Whole topics ("aggression", "attachment") cannot be argued in 4,000 words. Fix a behaviour, context, and specific evidence base.
- **Study-listing.** Summarising ten studies in turn is not analysis. Each study must earn its place by advancing or testing your argument.
- **Weak sources.** Relying on secondary summaries instead of the original peer-reviewed research thins your evaluation.
- **Ignoring counter-evidence.** A top essay engages with studies that contradict its line and explains why the balance falls where it does.

## How the Extended Essay is marked

The EE is externally assessed on **five criteria** worth **34 marks** in total, and it is graded **A–E**:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp, appropriately narrow research question and a clear research strategy.
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of the relevant psychological theories, studies, and terminology.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis, evaluation, and a sustained argument — this is where psychology essays are won or lost.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, formatting, and consistent academic citation.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your Reflections on Planning and Progress Form (RPPF).

Combined with your Theory of Knowledge grade, the EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus points** to your diploma. The finer marking detail differs by subject and by exam session, so treat the band descriptors as authoritative and check current requirements with your supervisor — you need a **supervisor qualified in psychology**. ## Frequently asked questions

### Can I do an experiment for my Psychology Extended Essay?

No. The psychology EE is a secondary-research, library-based essay. You are not permitted to collect primary data from human participants. You analyse and evaluate existing published studies and theories to answer your research question. Original experiments belong in the IA, not the EE.

### What makes a good Psychology EE research question?

One that is narrow, answerable from published peer-reviewed research, and evaluative. It should use a command term such as "to what extent", fix a specific behaviour and context, and set up a contrast or judgement — for example weighing a cognitive against a biological explanation of the same phenomenon.

### How is the Psychology EE different from the IA?

The IA is a small experiment you design and run yourself. The EE is a 4,000-word argument built from other researchers' published work, with no primary data collection. Different skills, different rules — do not blur them.

### How many sources should I use?

Quality over quantity, but a psychology EE typically leans on a solid set of peer-reviewed studies and named theories rather than textbooks. Enough to support and challenge your argument on both sides; your supervisor can advise on the range expected in your session.

### Does my supervisor have to teach Psychology?

You need a supervisor qualified in the subject of your essay. For a psychology EE that means someone able to guide you on psychological theory, evaluation, and academic conventions.

## Bottom line

A strong IB Psychology Extended Essay is narrow, argumentative, and built entirely from published research — never from your own experiment. Choose a topic where established studies or explanations disagree, sharpen it into an evaluative question with a command term, and let each source advance a judgement rather than pad a summary.
