The official grade boundaries for Cambridge International A-Level Further Mathematics (9231) for the 2026 exam series will be published on results day, 13 August 2026. These boundaries, or 'grade thresholds', are the minimum raw marks required to achieve each grade (A*, A, B, etc.) and are determined after all marking is complete. This article explains how these boundaries are set and how you can estimate your performance before they are released.

## How are 9231 Further Mathematics grades determined?
Your final grade in A-Level Further Mathematics is based on your performance across two papers. You will typically take Further Pure Mathematics 1 (Paper 1) and then choose one specialist paper from Further Mechanics (Paper 3) or Further Probability & Statistics (Paper 4). Each paper is marked out of a total raw mark (e.g., 75 for Paper 1, 50 for Papers 3 and 4). Your final grade is awarded based on the sum of your raw marks from these two components, which is known as your **aggregate mark**. Cambridge publishes grade thresholds for the individual papers and, crucially, for the overall subject award based on this aggregate score.

## Understanding raw marks vs. UMS
Unlike some UK-based exam boards that use a Uniform Mark Scale (UMS), Cambridge International qualifications are graded using **raw marks**. This is a more direct system: the mark you get on the paper is the mark that counts. Your task is to accumulate as many of these raw marks as possible. The marking scheme for 9231 is rigorous, using a system of B (independent), M (method), and A (accuracy) marks. Securing M marks is vital, as they are often awarded for demonstrating a correct method, even if your final answer is incorrect due to a calculation error. Long, chained proofs and multi-step problems mean that showing clear, logical working is essential for success.

## Why do grade boundaries change each year?
The primary reason grade boundaries fluctuate between exam sessions is to ensure fairness and maintain a consistent standard over time. Before setting the boundaries, senior examiners analyse student performance and the relative difficulty of the question papers. If a paper in a particular year is deemed more challenging than in previous years, the grade boundaries will be lowered to compensate. Conversely, if a paper is more straightforward, the boundaries may be raised. This process ensures that an A* grade in 2026 represents the same level of mathematical achievement as an A* in 2025 or 2024, regardless of minor variations in paper difficulty.

## When will the official 2026 grade boundaries be released?
For the June 2026 examination series, Cambridge will publish the official grade thresholds on results day, which is scheduled for **Thursday, 13 August 2026**. They are released simultaneously to schools and students, meaning there is no way to know the exact boundaries before this date. The thresholds document will detail the minimum raw marks needed for each grade for each individual paper, as well as for the overall subject options (e.g., the combination of Paper 1 and Paper 3). You can find these on the Cambridge International website on results day, and we will also analyse them on our page for [9231 on MarkScheme](/subjects/9231).

## How to estimate your 2026 grade
The most effective way to gauge your potential grade is to use past papers. Once you have completed a paper under timed, exam-like conditions, you should [mark your paper](/mark) meticulously using the official mark scheme. Be honest and strict with yourself, following the B, M, and A mark allocation precisely. Once you have a raw score for a component, compare it to the grade boundaries for that same component from the last two or three available exam sessions (e.g., June 2025, November 2024). This will give you a realistic indication of the grade you might have achieved. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is it true that Further Maths has very high grade boundaries?
Not necessarily. While the cohort of students taking Further Mathematics is exceptionally able, the examination papers are designed to be extremely challenging. Consequently, the raw mark percentage required for a top grade can sometimes be lower than in other A-Level subjects. Boundaries are set to reflect this difficulty, ensuring standards are maintained.

### What happens if I take a different combination of papers (e.g., Pure and Stats vs. Pure and Mechanics)?
Cambridge sets distinct overall grade boundaries for each valid combination of papers. For example, the aggregate boundary for the Pure/Mechanics option may differ slightly from the Pure/Statistics option in the same session. This ensures fairness by accounting for any minor differences in difficulty between the specialist papers that year.

### Can I predict the 2026 boundaries based on the 2025 ones?
You can use the 2025 boundaries as an important guide, but they are not a direct prediction. They are the most recent data point, but the exact difficulty of the 2026 papers is an unknown variable. It is far more reliable to look at a range from the last few exam series to understand the typical level required.
