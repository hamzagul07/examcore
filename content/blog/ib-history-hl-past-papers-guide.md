The most effective way to revise for IB History Higher Level is to master the assessment criteria for each paper and apply them through disciplined practice with past papers. Success isn't just about knowing historical facts; it's about demonstrating specific analytical skills in Paper 1's source analysis, Paper 2's thematic essays, and Paper 3's regional depth study, all while completing a high-quality Internal Assessment.

## Understanding the IB History HL Assessment
The IB History HL course is assessed through three external exam papers and one Internal Assessment (IA). **Paper 1** is a one-hour source-based paper on one of five prescribed subjects, testing your skills in source comprehension, analysis, and evaluation. **Paper 2** is a 90-minute essay paper where you answer two questions from two different world history topics. **Paper 3** is the HL-only component: a 2.5-hour exam requiring you to answer three essays on your chosen regional option, demanding significant depth of knowledge. Finally, the **Historical Investigation (IA)** is an independent research project of 2,200 words where you craft and answer your own historical question.

## The Role of Markbands in Getting a 7
Unlike exam boards that award marks for specific points, the IB uses **markbands** to assess your work. Each paper has a detailed rubric with level descriptors for different bands of achievement. To earn a 7, you must consistently produce work that meets the criteria of the top band. For an essay, this means moving beyond narrative to construct a “consistent and effective” argument, showing “well-developed” critical analysis, and evaluating different perspectives. Simply knowing your history is not enough; you must internalise the language of these markbands and ensure every paragraph you write helps push your response into that top level.

## How to Use IB History HL Past Papers Effectively
Passively reading [IB History past papers](/ib/past-papers/history-hl) is not an effective revision strategy. Instead, adopt a three-step cycle: work, mark, and drill. First, complete a full paper under timed exam conditions to simulate the real experience. Second, use the official IB markband descriptors to honestly assess your own work. Ask yourself: ‘Does my analysis show a “clear awareness” of the source’s value and limitations, or is it just a summary?’ This self-assessment is crucial for identifying your weaknesses. Finally, drill the skills you are lacking. If your Paper 2 essays are too descriptive, spend a session just planning analytical essay structures without writing them out in full. For targeted, expert evaluation, you can always [get feedback on your answer](/mark) to confirm your self-assessment and get actionable advice.

## Structuring Your Revision for Each Paper
Tailor your revision to the specific demands of each component. For **Paper 1**, focus on your prescribed subject and practise the formulaic question types, paying close attention to command terms like 'compare and contrast' and 'evaluate'. For **Paper 2**, ensure you have strong knowledge of two world history topics, with examples from different regions to support cross-regional arguments. Practise planning essays that directly address the question's command term. For **Paper 3**, your revision must be granular. You need deep, specific factual knowledge—names, dates, statistics, and historiography—for your chosen regional option. The questions are more focused than in Paper 2, and the markbands reward this greater detail.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them
A frequent mistake is writing a descriptive narrative instead of a focused analysis. Every sentence should contribute to answering the question, not just retelling a story. Another pitfall is ignoring the command terms; an 'evaluate' question requires a judgement on importance or success, while a 'discuss' question requires a balanced review of different factors or arguments. Finally, poor time management can undermine even the most knowledgeable student. The only way to avoid this is through repeated, timed practice with past papers to build stamina and pace. Vague generalisations will keep you in the middle markbands; top-level answers are built on a foundation of precise, relevant, and well-cited historical evidence.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between a Paper 2 and a Paper 3 essay?
Paper 2 essays are broad, thematic, and cross-regional, asking you to compare events and developments across the globe. Paper 3 essays are specific to your HL regional option and demand a much greater depth of detailed, factual knowledge. The scope is narrower, but the expectation for evidential support is significantly higher.

### How can I improve my source analysis for Paper 1?
Move beyond summarising what the sources say. For every source, explicitly analyse its **origin**, **purpose**, and **content** to make a judgement on its **value** and **limitations** with reference to the question. Practise the specific skills of comparison, contrast, and synthesis. The final question requires a mini-essay integrating the sources with your own knowledge.

### How many topics should I study for the exams?
For Paper 2, you must study two of the twelve world history topics in depth, as you will choose two questions from two different topic areas. For Paper 3, you must have comprehensive knowledge of one of the four regional options, covering all three required sections to give you maximum choice across the 18 available questions.

### When are the IB History exams and how are they graded?
The IB Diploma Programme exams are held in May and November each year. Your final grade for [IB History HL](/ib/subjects/history-hl) is awarded on a scale from 1 (lowest) to 7 (highest), which is a composite score based on your performance across all three exam papers and the Internal Assessment. Grade boundaries are set after each exam session.

## Bottom line
Success in IB History HL is a direct result of understanding the assessment objectives and practising the required skills until they become second nature. Use past papers not as a simple test of knowledge, but as a diagnostic tool to identify weaknesses in your analytical, evaluative, and writing skills. This methodical approach, focused on meeting the top markband criteria, is the most reliable path to a 7.
