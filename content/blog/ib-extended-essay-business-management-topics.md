A strong Business Management Extended Essay investigates **one real organisation**, applies **business theory and tools** — SWOT, Ansoff, ratio analysis, the marketing mix — and answers a **forward-looking, decision-oriented question**. The best topics are analytical and evaluative, not descriptive: you are not writing a profile of a company, you are using business frameworks to reach a **reasoned recommendation or judgement**. The one non-negotiable is access: if you cannot get enough data on the firm — primary or secondary — the essay will stall. Below are example research questions, including a "too broad → sharpened" contrast, plus how to narrow your own, the methodology examiners reward, and how it is marked.

This post is about *choosing and framing* a Business Management EE. For the mechanics that apply to every subject — the 4,000-word limit, criteria A–E, the RPPF — see the [IB Extended Essay guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## How the Business Management EE differs from the BM IA

Both apply the business toolkit to a real organisation, but they are different tasks:

- The **IA** is a research project on a real business, built around a proposal, primary and secondary data, and applied tools leading to a recommendation. See the [IB Business Management IA guide](/blog/ib-business-management-ia-guide).
- The **EE** is a single **4,000-word research essay** answering one focused question with **sustained analysis and evaluation**, deeper secondary research, and a more rigorous argument than the IA requires.

The IA can lean on a single well-run piece of primary research. The EE expects a fuller investigation: a sharp question, layered evidence, several tools applied to it, and a conclusion that genuinely weighs trade-offs and limitations.

## How to choose and narrow a research question

The classic Business Management EE failure is a question that invites *description* — "How successful is [company]?" — because the answer is a report, not an argument. Narrow along three axes:

- **A specific organisation and issue.** One firm, one decision or problem — a pricing change, an expansion, a new product line — not the whole company.
- **A specific tool or concept.** Ansoff, break-even, ratio analysis, the marketing mix, stakeholder analysis. The framework should be implied by the question.
- **A forward-looking or evaluative angle.** "Should [firm] do X?" or "To what extent did X improve Y?" forces judgement rather than narration.

Before you commit, confirm **you can get the data**. A brilliant question about a firm you cannot access financial or operational information on is a dead end.

## Example research questions

Use these as **templates for the level of focus to aim for**, not questions to copy. Choose an organisation you can actually gather data on — a local business you can interview, or a listed company with published accounts.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ | Tool / framework |
|---|---|---|
| How successful is [company]? | To what extent should [local café] pursue market development to grow revenue? | Ansoff matrix; break-even |
| Is [firm]'s marketing good? | How effective was [retailer]'s 2024 rebrand in improving customer perception in [city]? | Marketing mix; primary survey |
| Should the business expand? | Should [SME] finance its expansion through debt rather than retained profit? | Ratio analysis; sources of finance |
| What are [firm]'s strengths? | To what extent do [firm]'s internal weaknesses threaten its position against [rival]? | SWOT; competitive analysis |
| How does [company] motivate staff? | To what extent has flexible working improved staff retention at [organisation]? | Motivation theory; HR metrics |
| Is [firm] profitable? | Should [restaurant] cut its menu to improve net profit margin? | Contribution / break-even; margins |

Notice the pattern: each sharpened question names a **specific organisation, a decision or change, and points at a tool** — and most demand a recommendation. That is what separates analysis from a company profile.

## Subject-specific methodology and pitfalls

A Business Management EE must be **analytical and evaluative** — the tools do the arguing, not describing:

- **Apply frameworks to evidence**, never just define them. A SWOT that carries part of the argument beats a SWOT presented as a tidy box.
- **Combine primary and secondary data.** An interview with a manager, a customer survey, published accounts, and reputable industry reports together give you something to analyse and to evaluate for reliability.
- **Stay decision-oriented.** The strongest essays end in a **justified recommendation or judgement**, weighing feasibility, stakeholders, and risk.
- **Evaluate your own evidence.** Sample size, respondent bias, the limits of a public annual report, assumptions behind a forecast — acknowledging these is where evaluation marks live.
- **Keep the analysis current** — recent accounts, a recent decision — while grounding it in established business theory.

Common pitfalls that sink Business Management EEs:

- **A company description.** Narrating history, products, and structure with no argument is the single most common failure.
- **Tools defined, not applied.** A page explaining what Ansoff is, with no link to your firm's actual choices, earns little.
- **No access to data.** Choosing a firm you cannot get real information on forces vague, unsupported claims.
- **A question that cannot be answered** — too broad, or purely descriptive.
- **Recommendations with no feasibility.** "They should expand" without cost, risk, or stakeholder analysis is an opinion, not a conclusion.

## How the Business Management EE is marked

The EE is **externally marked out of 34** and graded **A–E**, on five criteria that apply across every subject:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp, decision-oriented research question and a sound methodology (your data sources and how you gathered them).
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of relevant business theory, tools, and the organisation's context.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis and evaluation — applying tools to your evidence, weighing trade-offs and limitations.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, tables and figures, citation, and staying within the 4,000 words.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your **RPPF** reflections with your supervisor.

Together with TOK, a strong EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus diploma points**. Exactly how tools, data, and evaluation are weighted within each criterion for Business Management is set out in the current subject-specific EE guidance, so **confirm the finer points with your supervisor**. ## Frequently asked questions

### Do I need primary research for a Business Management EE?

Primary data — an interview, a survey, first-hand observation — is highly valued because it gives you original evidence to analyse and to evaluate for reliability. It is not always mandatory, but an essay built only on public reports is harder to lift into the top bands. Whatever mix you use, confirm the current expectation with your supervisor, and follow your school's ethics guidance on consent for surveys and interviews.

### How do I stop it becoming a company description?

Anchor every section to your **research question and a tool**. If a paragraph could appear in the company's own "About us" page, cut it. Ask of each point: *what argument does this advance, and which framework am I applying?* A decision-oriented question — "should the firm do X?" — naturally forces analysis over narration.

### Can I write about a big public company or must it be local?

Either can work. A **listed company** gives you published accounts and abundant secondary data but no primary access; a **local business** may let you interview managers and run surveys but publishes little. Choose based on where you can actually get enough data to answer your question — access is the deciding factor.

### Does the EE subject have to match my HL Business Management?

It is recommended but not required — you need a **subject-qualified supervisor** in Business Management. Taking the subject at HL usually means stronger command of the tools, which helps criterion B, but a qualified supervisor is the formal requirement.

### How is it different from the Economics EE?

A Business Management EE analyses an **organisation's decisions** with business tools and aims at a recommendation; an Economics EE applies **economic theory and diagrams** to a market or macro question. If your argument is about strategy and management, it is Business Management; if it is about market mechanisms, it is Economics — see the [Economics EE topics guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-economics-topics).

## Bottom line

A great Business Management EE is not a company profile — it is a **narrow, decision-oriented question about a real organisation you can get data on**, answered by applying business tools to evidence and ending in a justified judgement. Confirm your access first, keep every section analytical, and evaluate your own data honestly.
