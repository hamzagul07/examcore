The official grade boundaries for the Cambridge International A-Level History (9489) 2026 exams will only be published on results day, which for the June 2026 series is 13 August 2026. These boundaries, or **grade thresholds**, are the minimum raw marks you need to achieve a certain grade (A, B, C, etc.) and are set *after* all papers have been marked. This guide explains how these boundaries are determined and how you can use past data to estimate your performance.

## What are grade boundaries?
Grade boundaries are the minimum number of raw marks a candidate needs to get a particular grade. They are not percentages set in advance. Instead, after marking is complete, senior examiners at Cambridge review student work from that specific exam session. They determine the mark that represents a 'just good enough for an A' standard, a 'just good enough for a C' standard, and so on. These marks then become the boundaries for that session, ensuring that the standard required for a grade remains consistent from one year to the next.

## How Cambridge sets 9489 History boundaries
For A-Level History, marking is based on a 'levels of response' system that rewards the quality of your argument, your use of specific supporting evidence, and, at the highest levels, your engagement with historiography. When setting boundaries, examiners analyse the overall performance of the cohort. They identify scripts that exemplify the required standard for each grade, based on the descriptors in the mark scheme. The raw scores on these benchmark scripts then inform where the final **grade thresholds** are set for each paper, ensuring fairness regardless of whether a particular exam paper was slightly more or less demanding than in previous years.

## Understanding the components of A-Level History (9489)
The 9489 syllabus is made up of several **components**, typically a document-based paper and one or more essay papers, depending on the options you have studied. Cambridge publishes grade boundaries for each individual component as well as for the overall qualification. This allows you to see your performance on each paper, but your final A-Level grade is determined by the sum of your weighted marks across all the components you have taken. A weaker performance on one paper can be compensated for by a stronger performance on another.

## Why grade boundaries change each session
The primary reason grade boundaries fluctuate between exam sessions is to account for variations in the difficulty of the examination papers. For example, if the document source in Paper 1 is found to be unusually complex, the grade boundary for an A on that paper might be set a few marks lower than last year. Conversely, if an essay paper is deemed more straightforward, the boundary might be slightly higher. This adjustment process is a crucial part of maintaining a fair and consistent standard over time, meaning your grade reflects your historical skills, not the specific paper you sat.

## How to estimate your 2026 grade
While you cannot know the 2026 boundaries in advance, you can make a reasonable estimate by comparing your performance against recent historical data. The best method is to complete past papers under timed conditions and mark them strictly using the official mark scheme. Then, compare your raw marks against the grade boundaries for the same components from the last two or three available exam sessions (e.g., June 2025, November 2025, and June 2024). ## Frequently asked questions

### When will the official 2026 grade boundaries be released?
The official grade thresholds are released on results day. For the June 2026 exam series, this is scheduled for Thursday, 13 August 2026. They are published on the Cambridge International website at the same time that schools and centres are able to access student results through the secure site.

### Is it true that an A is always 80%?
No, this is a common myth. Cambridge qualifications do not use a fixed percentage scale for grades. The raw mark needed for an A (or any other grade) is determined after marking and depends on the difficulty of the paper. It could be higher or lower than 80% of the total marks.

### How can I improve my chances of getting a top grade?
Focus on mastering the skills outlined in the mark scheme. For 9489 History, this means constructing well-supported, analytical arguments and evaluating sources and interpretations, not just describing events. Regularly practising past papers and getting detailed feedback is vital. You can gain insight by trying to [mark a paper](/mark) yourself against the official criteria.

### Where can I find past grade boundaries for 9489 History?
Cambridge publishes a 'Grade Threshold' document for every exam series, which is available on their public website. ## Bottom line
Don't become preoccupied with trying to guess the exact grade boundaries for 2026. Instead, concentrate your efforts on honing the historical skills that the examiners are looking for: analysis, evaluation, and the effective use of evidence. Use past boundaries as a guide to understand the standard required, but your ultimate goal should be to maximise your raw mark by producing the highest quality work you can.
