Take **HL** in IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science (SEHS) if it is central to your university plans — sports science, physiotherapy, kinesiology, exercise physiology, or a related health-science pathway — and you genuinely enjoy the anatomy, physiology, and data work. Take **SL** if SEHS is there for breadth and to satisfy your Group 4 requirement rather than as the foundation of your degree. So the honest answer is: it depends on whether SEHS sits at the centre of your plans or off to the side. Below is what actually differs between the two levels, how much harder HL really is, and a framework for deciding. Because the syllabus was recently revised, confirm the exact current paper structure in your IB subject guide and with your teacher.

## What's the same at SL and HL
Both levels are the **same interdisciplinary Group 4 science**, drawing on **anatomy and physiology, energy systems, biomechanics, skill acquisition and motor learning, nutrition, and sport and exercise psychology** — the science of the human body in sport, exercise and health. SL and HL students study the same core strands and are assessed in the same style: **multiple-choice**, **data-based and short-answer**, and **extended-response** questions.

Both also complete the **same scientific-investigation Internal Assessment** — a collaborative sciences project alongside an individual investigation, the standard Group 4 sciences IA, marked against identical criteria at either level. The IA is not scaled up for HL; the workload difference lives in the taught content and the exams.

## What HL adds
HL is not a different subject — it is the same strands taught in **greater depth**, plus **HL-only content** that SL students never cover. In practice that means more physiology and mechanism-level detail, more demanding data analysis, and more extended-response work in longer, tougher papers. The exact additional topics, paper count, durations, and weightings changed in the recent revision, so treat the table below as the *shape* of the difference and **check the current subject guide** for specifics.

|  | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Strands covered | Core interdisciplinary strands | Same strands |
| Depth of content | Core treatment | Same core **plus** HL-only topics and greater depth |
| Question types | MCQ, data-based/short-answer, extended-response | Same, with more extended-response demand |
| Exams | Shorter papers | Longer, more demanding papers |
| Recall / detail load | High | Higher — larger volume, finer detail |
| Internal Assessment | Scientific investigation | **Same** scientific investigation |

The extra HL hours are not padding — they are new material and deeper versions of shared topics, examined with longer papers and more synthesis. For the technique side of scoring well at either level, see [how to get a 7 in IB SEHS](/blog/ib-sehs-how-to-get-a-7).

## How much harder is HL SEHS?
SEHS is **content-and-precision-heavy at both levels**; the honest difficulty gap is mostly about **volume and depth**. HL asks you to hold a larger web of named structures, mechanisms, and terms in memory, to apply them under exam pressure, and to handle more extended-response and data work. It blends **biology-style recall** with **applied data analysis** and some **calculation** in energy systems and biomechanics — memory-and-application-hard, but light on heavy algebra.

If you enjoy the subject and revise consistently, HL is very doable. If SEHS is a subject you tolerate rather than like, the extra recall and depth can feel relentless, and that effort may be better spent on other HLs central to your plans. For a fuller look at what makes it tough, see [is IB SEHS hard](/blog/ib-sehs-hard).

## University fit
SEHS is a respected Group 4 science, but how it counts for a given degree varies:

- **Sports science, exercise physiology, kinesiology, sports coaching** — SEHS is directly relevant and HL is often welcomed or preferred; check whether a specific HL science is named.
- **Physiotherapy, sports therapy, nutrition, health science** — requirements vary; some accept SEHS, some prefer or require HL Biology or HL Chemistry instead. Confirm exactly what each course names.
- **Medicine, dentistry, biosciences** — these usually require HL Biology and/or HL Chemistry specifically, and **SEHS may not substitute** for them. If any of these are on your list, plan the required HL sciences rather than assuming SEHS covers you.

The critical rule: **check the exact entry requirements** for the specific courses and countries you are considering, because offers differ between universities and change year to year. ## Who should take HL vs SL

**Take HL SEHS if you:**

- Are aiming at sports science, exercise physiology, kinesiology, or a related health-science degree that values it
- Genuinely enjoy anatomy, physiology, and interpreting exercise data
- Can commit consistent revision time to a content-heavy subject
- Have confirmed HL SEHS is accepted (or preferred) for your target courses

**Take SL SEHS if you:**

- Want SEHS for breadth and to meet your Group 4 requirement
- Are directing your HL energy into other subjects central to your plans
- Prefer a solid grounding over the extra depth and volume of HL
- Have confirmed your target courses accept SL SEHS — or don't require a specific HL science instead

## How to decide
1. **Start from your degree, not the subject.** List target courses and read their published IB requirements.
2. **Check whether they name a specific HL science.** If medicine or biosciences appear, they usually want HL Biology or Chemistry — SEHS may not substitute.
3. **Be honest about interest and capacity.** HL rewards students who enjoy the physiology and data detail; it punishes reluctant memorisers.
4. **Weigh your whole HL package.** You take three HLs — make sure SEHS HL earns its place against your other priorities.
5. **Preview real content.** Skim the SL and HL courses and try a few questions before locking in.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can SEHS replace HL Biology or Chemistry for medicine?
Usually no. Medicine, dentistry, and most biosciences require HL Biology and/or HL Chemistry by name, and SEHS typically does not substitute. If those courses are on your list, take the specific HL sciences they require and always confirm against each university's published requirements.

### Is HL SEHS hard?
It is demanding mainly because of **volume and depth** — more content, finer detail, and more extended-response and data work. Individual topics are manageable, and the maths is light. With genuine interest and steady active recall, HL is very achievable.

### How much more content does HL cover?
HL adds HL-only topics and greater depth across the shared strands, examined in longer, more demanding papers. The exact additional content and weightings changed in the recent syllabus revision — check the current subject guide for specifics.

### Is the IA different at HL?
No. Both levels complete the same Group 4 scientific-investigation IA, marked against the same criteria. Only the taught content and exams differ between the levels.

### Is SEHS a "soft" science?
No — it is a full Group 4 experimental science with real physiology, biomechanics, data analysis, and calculation. It is marked point-per-mark like the other sciences and rewards precise terminology, not general sports knowledge.

## Bottom line
IB Sports, Exercise and Health Science is the **same interdisciplinary strands and the same scientific-investigation IA** at both levels — HL simply adds HL-only topics, more depth, longer exams, and a heavier recall-and-application load. If a sports- or health-science degree that values SEHS is the plan, HL is the sensible choice; if SEHS is for breadth and your Group 4 requirement, SL delivers a solid grounding. Crucially, if you are aiming at medicine or biosciences, confirm whether HL Biology or Chemistry is required instead.
