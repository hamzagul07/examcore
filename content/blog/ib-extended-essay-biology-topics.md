The strongest IB Biology Extended Essay topic is a **focused biological investigation with a measurable variable** — ideally an experimental study you can run safely with school apparatus, or a well-justified database study where the analysis carries the marks. Your research question should name one thing you change and one thing you measure, sit clearly inside biological theory (enzyme kinetics, osmosis, ecology, physiology), and pass a hard **safety and ethics** check. A narrow local or lab system almost always beats a sweeping "does X affect Y everywhere" question. This post shows how to turn an interest into an examinable RQ, with an example table and the methodology traps that sink EEs. For criteria in full, see the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## Biology EE vs Biology IA — what's different

The Internal Assessment and the Extended Essay share DNA — both are individual investigations linked to biological theory — but the EE is a different animal in **scale and independence**. The IA is around ten hours of work and a report of roughly 6–12 pages, marked on five IA criteria. The EE is a **4,000-word** research essay marked on five *EE* criteria (A–E) by an external examiner, and it demands a genuine argument built across the whole piece, not just a clean data table. Where the IA rewards a tidy method, the EE rewards **sustained critical thinking**: you interpret your data against published biology, weigh limitations honestly, and reach a reasoned conclusion. If you have already written a Biology IA, treat that as the warm-up — the [IB Biology IA guide](/blog/ib-biology-ia-guide) covers the shared groundwork.

## What makes a strong Biology EE research question

A good Biology EE RQ does five things:

- **Focused** — one independent variable, one dependent variable, one clearly bounded system. Not "what affects plant growth" but "how does *nitrate concentration* affect *root length* in *germinating cress*".
- **Measurable** — the DV produces quantitative data you can plot, average and analyse, not impressions you judge by eye.
- **Grounded in biology** — you can explain the expected trend from theory (limiting factors, enzyme denaturation, osmosis, homeostasis). Theory is what lifts data collection into an *investigation*.
- **Feasible** — the equipment, organisms, time and access exist. A local pond, a school water bath or a public dataset beats an idea that needs a research lab.
- **Safe and ethical** — no invasive human work, no unethical animal procedures, no dangerous chemistry, no habitat damage. If it can't clear this bar, change it.

## Choosing and narrowing your topic

Start from something you genuinely find interesting — that feeds Criterion E (engagement). Then narrow it in three moves. **Pick a system you can actually access**: potato tissue, pondweed, yeast, seeds, a local transect, your own non-invasive physiology, or a public database. **Choose one variable to change and one to measure**, so "exercise and health" becomes "effect of step-up rate on heart-rate recovery time". Finally, **set a range and controls** — about five levels of the IV, three or more repeats, and a written list of the variables you will hold constant. A broad sweep across many conditions reads as unfocused; a single tightly controlled question reads as rigorous.

If you cannot run a controlled experiment — the organism is protected, the timescale is too long, or the ethics don't clear — a **secondary-data or database study** is a legitimate route. Because you are then marked on what you do with the numbers rather than on lab technique, the analytical angle has to be even sharper: a real statistical test, a modelled relationship, a genuine biological interpretation. A spreadsheet graph with no processing will not score.

## Example research questions: too broad to sharpened

Treat these as **starting points**, not copy-paste answers — an RQ identical to an online example undermines authenticity.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
|---|---|
| Does temperature affect enzymes? | How does temperature (10–70 °C) affect the rate of catalase activity in potato, measured as oxygen produced per minute? |
| What affects photosynthesis? | How does light intensity (lamp distance) affect the rate of photosynthesis in *Elodea*, measured as oxygen bubbles per minute? |
| Does salt harm plants? | How does soil NaCl concentration (0–200 mM) affect the germination rate and root length of cress seeds? |
| Does exercise affect the heart? | How does step-up exercise intensity affect heart-rate recovery time in briefed adult volunteers? |
| Is biodiversity affected by humans? | How does distance from a footpath affect plant species diversity along a transect, measured with a diversity index from quadrat data? |
| Do antibacterials work? | How does garlic extract concentration affect the zone of inhibition of a school-approved bacterial culture, in millimetres? |

Each sharpened RQ names an IV with a range, a measurable DV with units, and a bounded system — and each links straight to a chunk of biology you can argue from.

## Methodology and pitfalls

A Biology EE lives or dies on its method. Design for **controlled variables** first: list every factor that could confound your DV (temperature, volume, concentration, time, light) and state how you hold each constant. Collect **enough data** — around five IV levels with three-plus repeats — so you can plot means with error bars and justify a statistical test (a *t*-test for two means, a correlation coefficient, a chi-squared for categorical counts) rather than sprinkling statistics for show. **Pilot the method once** and adjust the range if your apparatus can't detect the effect.

The pitfalls examiners see every year:

- **Too broad** — a question that needs several investigations, so nothing is answered in depth.
- **No measurable DV** — a "healthier-looking plant" that can't be quantified.
- **Thin theory** — a topic you can't explain biologically has nothing to evaluate against.
- **Ethics ignored** — invasive human procedures, ingestion beyond everyday levels, unethical animal work, or damaging a habitat. Human volunteers need informed consent, the right to withdraw, and anonymised data.
- **Descriptive, not argued** — presenting data without interpreting it against published biology. The EE wants an argument.
- **Correlation as causation** — especially in database studies, where a trend is not a mechanism.

## How the Extended Essay is marked

The EE is marked out of roughly **34 marks** across five criteria and graded on an **A–E** scale:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp RQ and a sound, well-justified methodology.
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of the relevant biology, used accurately.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis, argument and evaluation — the heaviest lifting.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, formatting, referencing and the word limit.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your **RPPF** (Reflections on Planning and Progress Form).

Your EE grade combines with TOK to award up to **3 bonus diploma points**. Your essay must be supervised by a subject-qualified supervisor, and Biology-specific band detail is best read against the [IB Extended Essay complete guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide) and your school's subject brief rather than memorised second-hand.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does my Biology EE have to be an experiment?

No — an experimental investigation is ideal because it gives you original, controlled data, but a well-justified **database or secondary-data study** is allowed and can score highly. The catch is that the analysis then has to be genuinely strong: a real statistical or modelling angle, not a graph pulled from a spreadsheet.

### How is the Biology EE different from the Biology IA?

Scale and independence. The IA is around ten hours and 6–12 pages on five IA criteria; the EE is a **4,000-word** essay on five EE criteria (A–E), externally marked, demanding a sustained argument rather than a clean data table. See the [Biology IA guide](/blog/ib-biology-ia-guide) for the shared foundations.

### Can I use human participants in a Biology EE?

Yes, for **non-invasive** measures like heart rate, reaction time or blood pressure — but only with informed consent, the right to withdraw, anonymised data, and nothing risky or ingested beyond everyday levels. Anything invasive is not permitted.

### How many marks is the Extended Essay worth?

The EE is marked out of roughly **34 marks** across criteria A–E and graded **A–E**. Combined with TOK it can add up to **3 bonus points** to your diploma total.

### How narrow should my research question be?

Narrow enough that one clear variable answers it in depth. "How does temperature affect catalase activity in potato?" works as an EE; "what affects enzymes?" is a whole topic, not a research question.

## Bottom line

A strong Biology Extended Essay starts with a modest, measurable, well-controlled question rooted in biological theory — one IV, one DV, a sensible range, real repeats, and an argument you build across 4,000 words. Pick a system you can access and an area you care about, then sharpen it until it fits the *How does X affect Y of Z?* template and clears the safety and ethics check.
