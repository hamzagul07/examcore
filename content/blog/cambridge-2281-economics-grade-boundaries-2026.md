The grade boundaries for the Cambridge O-Level Economics 2281 exam in 2026 will be the minimum raw marks you need to achieve a specific grade (from A* to E). These are not predetermined percentages and will only be finalised and published by Cambridge International after all the exam papers have been marked for the June and November sessions. The exact thresholds are set to ensure that the standard required for each grade remains consistent from one year to the next.

## What are grade boundaries?
A **grade boundary**, also known as a grade threshold, is the minimum raw mark a candidate must score on a paper, or in total for a qualification, to be awarded a particular grade. For 2281 Economics, Cambridge will set separate thresholds for Paper 1 and Paper 2, as well as an overall threshold for the final grade. These are not percentages; for example, a grade A will not be set at a fixed 80% of the total marks. Instead, senior examiners review student performance across the board and set the boundaries to reflect a consistent level of academic achievement.

## Why do grade boundaries change each year?
Grade boundaries are adjusted for every exam session to ensure fairness. The core principle is that it should be no more or less difficult to achieve a certain grade in one year than it was in any other year. If the questions in the 2026 papers are found to be slightly more challenging than the 2025 papers, the grade boundaries will likely be lowered to compensate. Conversely, if a paper is considered slightly more straightforward, the boundaries may be raised. This process ensures that your final grade reflects your understanding of economics, not how lucky you were with the difficulty of a particular paper.

## How are 2281 Economics papers graded?
Your final grade in O-Level Economics is based on your performance across two papers, which test a range of skills. Examiners award marks for different Assessment Objectives: demonstrating knowledge of economic definitions and theory, applying this knowledge to specific scenarios, analysing economic issues using tools like diagrams, and evaluating economic arguments and policies. To score highly, you must go beyond simple definitions and show you can explain concepts, interpret data, and construct well-reasoned arguments with applied examples. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is an A grade always 80%?
No, this is a common misconception. Grades in Cambridge exams are not based on fixed percentages. The raw mark required for an A (or any other grade) fluctuates each year based on the difficulty of the paper. An A represents a standard of achievement, not a percentage score.

### Can I predict the exact 2026 grade boundaries?
It is impossible to predict the exact boundaries. They are only determined after all marking is complete and senior examiners have analysed student performance. The best you can do is create an informed estimate by comparing your practice paper scores against the boundaries from the last two or three exam sessions.
