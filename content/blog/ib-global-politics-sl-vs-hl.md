Take **HL** if you are drawn to politics, international relations, law, or the humanities and want the deeper, case-study-driven version of the subject; take **SL** if you want a strong grounding in global politics alongside a different specialism. Both levels study the same four core concepts and complete the same engagement-project internal assessment — the honest answer to "SL or HL?" depends less on how much you like the subject and more on your **degree plans** and whether you want the extra depth the HL extension demands. Here is a precise comparison. (Exact timings and weightings shift between sessions, so confirm specifics in the current IB subject guide and with your teacher.)

## What's the same at SL and HL

Both levels are built on the **same four core concepts**, studied through a "people, power and politics" lens with a required real-world political-issue focus:

- **Power, sovereignty and international relations**
- **Human rights**
- **Development**
- **Peace and conflict**

Both SL and HL students sit source-based **Paper 1** and extended-response **Paper 2**, and both complete the **same engagement project** — the internal assessment in which you engage with a real political issue and write an analytical report linking your experience to political theory and concepts. The IA task is common to both levels, so the coursework experience is broadly similar.

The difference is not *which* concepts you study — it is the additional HL extension and case-study work layered on top.

## What HL adds

HL keeps all of the SL material — the four core concepts, both papers, and the engagement project — then adds the **HL extension**: a study of global political challenges. These are assessed through **case studies presented orally or as video** rather than as a written exam. So HL students take on an extra body of content and an extra, distinctly different assessment format on top of everything SL students do.

Here is the shape of the difference:

| | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Core concepts | Power/sovereignty, human rights, development, peace & conflict | Same four core concepts |
| Paper 1 (sources) | Yes | Yes |
| Paper 2 (essays) | Yes | Yes |
| Engagement project (IA) | Yes (same task) | Yes (same task) |
| HL extension | — | **Global political challenges, via case studies (oral/video)** |
| Content and depth | Core treatment | Core **plus** the HL extension and its case studies |
| Teaching hours | Fewer | More |

The core SL-to-HL difference, in one line: **HL adds the HL extension and its case studies.** Everything else is shared. Confirm the exact assessment format, timings, and requirements for your session in the current subject guide and with your teacher.

## How much harder is HL Global Politics?

Honestly: **HL is a clear step up**, but not because the core subject becomes more abstract. The core skills — building an argument, evaluating from multiple perspectives, applying concepts, and using real examples — are the same at both levels. The added difficulty comes from concrete places:

- **The HL extension content** — global political challenges are real additional material to learn, research, and analyse.
- **A different assessment skill** — the case studies are presented orally or as video, which asks for research, structuring, and presentation on top of essay writing.
- **More to carry** — a larger body of content and an extra deliverable alongside the shared papers and engagement project.

None of this is conceptually hard in the way HL Maths AA can be. The challenge is **breadth, research, and the extra case-study work**, not raw abstraction. If you enjoy current affairs, argue in structured points, and are willing to research real cases in depth, HL is very manageable. ## Who should take HL vs SL

**Take HL Global Politics if you:**

- Are heading towards politics, international relations, law, development, or a humanities/social-science degree
- Genuinely enjoy current affairs and analysing real political issues in depth
- Are comfortable researching case studies and presenting your analysis
- Want to keep the widest range of related university options open

**Take SL Global Politics if you:**

- Want a strong grounding in global politics alongside a different HL specialism
- Are directing your HL energy into other subjects central to your plans
- Prefer the core course without the extra HL extension and case-study workload
- Have confirmed your target courses do not require the subject at HL

Neither choice is "less serious" — a confident SL 7 is worth far more than a stretched HL 5.

## University relevance

Politics, international relations, and law degrees generally **value** IB Global Politics, but they **rarely strictly require it at HL**. Most such courses ask for a strong overall diploma and specific grades in whatever HL subjects you offer, and many value essay-writing humanities broadly rather than this subject specifically. So HL Global Politics is a relevant, helpful HL for these paths — but it is usually not a gatekeeping requirement the way HL Maths can be for engineering.

The practical rule: **check the actual entry requirements** for the specific courses and universities you are targeting. Requirements differ between institutions and countries and change year to year. Do not pick HL purely because you assume a politics degree demands it — verify against official course pages, and preview the real content through the free [Global Politics SL course](/ib/courses/global-politics-sl) and [HL course](/ib/courses/global-politics-hl) before you commit.

## How to decide

1. **Start from your degree, not the subject.** List target courses and read their published IB requirements.
2. **Check whether HL is genuinely needed.** For most politics/IR/law courses it is valued but not strictly required at HL — confirm rather than assume.
3. **Be honest about interest and capacity.** HL rewards students who enjoy researching real political issues; the HL extension is a chore if the subject is only a filler.
4. **Weigh your whole HL package.** You take three HLs — make sure Global Politics HL earns its place against your other priorities.
5. **Preview real content.** Skim the SL and HL courses and try a few questions with the [SL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/global-politics-sl) and [HL topic practice](/ib/past-papers/global-politics-hl) before locking in.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the main difference between SL and HL Global Politics?

The core is identical — the same four concepts, both papers, and the same engagement project. HL adds the **HL extension**: a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies presented orally or as video. That extension is the defining SL-to-HL difference.

### Is HL Global Politics hard?

It is a step up from SL, mainly because of the extra HL-extension content and the case-study work, plus a larger body to revise. The core thinking is the same style as SL — argument, evaluation, concepts, examples — with more to carry. It is very manageable if you enjoy the subject and research consistently.

### Do SL and HL do the same internal assessment?

Yes. Both levels complete the same engagement project — you engage with a real political issue and write an analytical report linking your experience to theory and concepts. The IA task is common to both.

### Do I need HL Global Politics for a politics or law degree?

Usually not strictly. These courses value the subject but rarely require it at HL, often preferring a strong overall diploma and essay-writing humanities generally. Always check the exact requirements for your target courses and countries.

### Can I move from HL to SL later?

Often yes, and it is generally the easier direction because the SL course is the shared core without the HL extension. Moving up from SL to HL mid-course is harder. Decide with your teacher, and sooner rather than later.

## Bottom line

SL and HL Global Politics share the same four core concepts, the same two papers, and the same engagement-project IA — the real difference is HL's added extension, a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies. Choose **HL** if you are aiming at politics, international relations, law, or a related humanities degree and want the deeper, research-driven version, and **SL** if you want a solid grounding alongside another specialism.
