If you are weighing up IB Global Politics, here is the honest verdict up front: it is rarely the content that trips students up — it is the writing. The hard part is building a clear argument, evaluating an issue from more than one perspective, and using the course's key concepts precisely, all under exam time pressure. Description is easy; the marks live in evaluation. That makes Global Politics argument-hard and precision-hard rather than memory-hard or maths-hard — good news if you like current affairs and reasoning, and a warning if you were hoping to coast on opinions. Below is a balanced look at what makes it tough, what makes it manageable, and how to make a 7 realistic. (Exact assessment specifics shift between sessions, so confirm them in the current IB subject guide and with your teacher.)

## Is IB Global Politics hard? The honest answer

Compared with the sciences or Maths, Global Politics asks almost nothing of your algebra and very little rote recall. Most students find the content genuinely interesting — power, sovereignty, human rights, development, peace and conflict, all tied to real events. The difficulty lives in the *writing*: two students can "know" the same issue and score very differently because one builds a balanced, evaluated argument in the examiner's language and the other writes a fluent description or a one-sided opinion. So "hard" here means demanding argument and precision with concepts — not maths or memorisation.

## What makes IB Global Politics challenging

**Evaluation over description.** The markbands reward a clear thesis, balanced multi-perspective analysis, and a reasoned judgement — not a well-written account of what happened. Description caps you in the middle bands however fluent it is. Learning to *weigh* rather than *tell* is the central challenge.

**Balance and multiple perspectives.** Strong answers hold several viewpoints at once — different states, institutions, theoretical lenses, or stakeholders — and set them against each other before reaching a verdict. A one-sided answer, however confident, never opens the top evaluation band.

**Concepts and examples used, not named.** Power, sovereignty, human rights, development, and peace and conflict should work as lenses that sharpen the argument, not background theory to name and move past. The subject also demands specific, contemporary examples used to advance the point, not generic references to "some countries" — building and updating a bank of precise cases takes ongoing effort.

**Command-term precision.** "Describe" and "outline" want different things from "evaluate," "discuss," and "to what extent." Answer the wrong command term and even good political knowledge loses marks — see [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained). And in Paper 1, the source-based paper rewards comparing and evaluating stimulus material rather than paraphrasing it, which is a distinct skill from essay writing.

## What makes IB Global Politics manageable

**Little to no heavy maths.** If equations are your weak spot, this subject is forgiving — the demands are on reasoning and writing, not calculation.

**Interesting, connected content.** The four core concepts link to real, current events, so the material tends to stick because you care about it. Following the news becomes revision, and issues reinforce each other across the concepts.

**A well-defined skill to drill.** Because the challenge is argument and evaluation, the fixes are clear: practise structured, balanced essays and mark them against the bands. Progress tends to come quickly once you switch from writing everything you know to writing to the markband. The four concepts also give you a ready-made lens for almost any issue, so you rarely start an answer from a blank page.

## SL vs HL — how much harder is HL?

HL is a clear step up from SL, and the main reason is the **HL extension**: a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies presented orally or as video. That is extra content plus a different assessment skill — research and presentation — layered on top of the shared papers and engagement project. The core thinking is the same style at both levels; there is simply more of it, and the case-study work is a distinct demand. If you are strong on the subject and enjoy researching real cases, HL is very doable; if it is a supporting subject you find heavy going at SL, be realistic about the added load. For a fuller breakdown see [IB Global Politics SL vs HL](/blog/ib-global-politics-sl-vs-hl).

## Is a 7 achievable?

Yes — and the path is clearer than in many subjects, precisely because the challenge is so well defined. A 7 comes from disciplined habits: **structured, balanced essays** that argue from multiple perspectives, **explicit use of the key concepts**, **specific real examples**, and a **reasoned judgement** that answers the exact question. Students who write to the markband rather than describing everything they know are the ones who convert interest into top grades. There is no hidden barrier — do the right writing practice and the grade follows. For the full method see [how to get a 7 in IB Global Politics](/blog/ib-global-politics-how-to-get-a-7).

## Who tends to find it hard vs easy

**Tends to find it easier:** students who enjoy current affairs and debate, who like structuring arguments, who can hold two sides of an issue at once, and who read questions carefully. Strong essay writers with an eye for balance have a real edge.

**Tends to find it harder:** students who write fluent description without evaluating, who argue one side confidently and stop, who name concepts without using them, or who ignore command terms. Interestingly, being weak at maths is not the problem here — under-practising evaluation is.

## How to make IB Global Politics easier

An action plan that targets what actually gets marked:

- **Practise evaluation, not description.** End every essay with a supported judgement, write in the counter-view before your verdict, and make each paragraph advance an argument rather than tell a story.
- **Use concepts as lenses.** For each issue, ask which of the four core concepts sharpens the analysis, and weave it in explicitly.
- **Build an example bank.** Keep specific, current cases across the four concepts, and update them as events move.
- **Drill command terms and sources.** Practise "evaluate/discuss/to what extent," and practise comparing Paper 1 sources rather than paraphrasing them.
- **Mark against the bands.** Write answers, then score them honestly against the descriptors to see exactly where you dropped a level.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is IB Global Politics HL hard?

HL is harder than SL, mainly because of the HL extension — a study of global political challenges assessed through case studies — plus more content to carry. The core thinking is the same style as SL, with the added research and presentation demand of the case studies. Manageable with steady essay practice and criterion marking, especially if you enjoy the subject. See [IB Global Politics SL vs HL](/blog/ib-global-politics-sl-vs-hl).

### Is Global Politics easier than the sciences?

For most students the maths and rote-recall burden is much lighter, so if calculations are your weakness, it can feel easier. But it demands strong, balanced essay writing and precise use of concepts. "Easier" depends on whether your strength is reasoning-and-writing (Global Politics suits you) or quantitative problem-solving.

### Do I need to be good at maths for IB Global Politics?

No. The subject is built on argument, evaluation, and concepts, not calculation. Comfort with structuring a balanced essay matters far more than any maths ability.

### Why do students lose marks even when they know the topic?

Almost always because answers describe rather than evaluate, argue one side, or name concepts without using them. The markbands want a balanced, multi-perspective argument with real examples and a reasoned judgement — practising to the band fixes this, and it is the same habit that makes a 7 achievable. See [how to get a 7 in IB Global Politics](/blog/ib-global-politics-how-to-get-a-7).

## Bottom line

IB Global Politics is not maths-hard or memory-hard; it is argument-hard and precision-hard. The real challenge is evaluating an issue from multiple perspectives, using the four core concepts precisely, and grounding every point in a real example — and all of that responds directly to structured essay practice and honest marking against the bands. There is little calculation, the content is genuinely interesting, and a 7 is achievable with disciplined work. Treat every answer as a chance to evaluate rather than describe, mark yourself like an examiner, and the subject becomes far more manageable than its reputation suggests.
