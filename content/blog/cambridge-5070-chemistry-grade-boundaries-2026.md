The official grade boundaries for the Cambridge O-Level Chemistry 5070 examination in 2026 will not be known until results day. Cambridge International sets these thresholds *after* all exam papers have been marked, a process designed to ensure that the standard required to achieve a certain grade remains consistent from one year to the next. Therefore, any specific numbers you see before results day are purely speculation.

## What are grade boundaries?
A **grade boundary**, often called a grade threshold, is the minimum raw mark a candidate must score on a paper, or in total, to be awarded a specific grade. For example, there will be a minimum mark for an A, a minimum mark for a B, and so on. Cambridge publishes these for each component paper (e.g., Paper 2 Theory, Paper 4 Alternative to Practical) and for the overall subject qualification. These are not decided in advance; they are a key part of the post-examination awarding process.

## How are 5070 Chemistry boundaries determined?
After the marking of all 5070 Chemistry papers is complete, a panel of senior examiners and assessment experts convenes. They analyse how students performed on each question, considering the overall difficulty of the examination compared to previous sessions. For a subject like O-Level Chemistry, with its strong emphasis on applying knowledge of bonding, equations, and qualitative analysis, they look closely at whether the papers effectively differentiated between students of different abilities. The final boundaries are then set to ensure that a student who achieves a grade 'A' in 2026 has demonstrated the same level of chemical understanding as a student who achieved an 'A' in a previous year.

## Why do grade boundaries change each year?
Grade boundaries for 5070 Chemistry fluctuate between exam sessions for one primary reason: to ensure fairness. It is impossible to create exam papers of the exact same difficulty year after year. If a particular year's Paper 2 is found to be slightly more demanding than the previous year's, the grade boundaries for that paper may be lowered to compensate. Conversely, if a paper is deemed more straightforward, the boundaries might be raised. This adjustment is a crucial quality control measure, ensuring that your final grade reflects your subject mastery, not the relative difficulty of the specific paper you sat.

## Frequently asked questions

### Will the 2026 boundaries be higher or lower than 2025?
It is impossible to predict whether the 2026 boundaries will be higher or lower. The final thresholds depend entirely on an expert analysis of student performance across the globe and the relative difficulty of the papers. They are adjusted specifically to maintain a consistent standard, so they could move in either direction.

### Is there a fixed percentage of students who get an A*?
No, Cambridge does not operate a 'norm-referenced' system that awards an A* to a fixed quota of top students. The system is 'criterion-referenced', meaning grades are awarded for demonstrating a specific level of knowledge and skill. If more students meet the standard for an A* in a given year, more students will receive it.

### What's the difference between a component and an overall threshold?
A component threshold is the minimum mark needed for a grade on a single paper, like Paper 2. The overall threshold is the minimum total mark required for a grade in the O-Level qualification as a whole, after the scores from each component have been weighted and combined. Achieving a certain grade in one component does not guarantee the same grade overall.
