Take **HL French B** if you already have a solid command of French, enjoy reading in the language, and want the stretch of studying literature — or if a course or university you are eyeing values a higher-level language. Take **SL** if French is there for breadth and balance in your diploma, or if you are building confidence and want to focus your HL energy on subjects central to your plans. The honest answer is: it depends on your current French level, your appetite for reading two literary works, and where the language sits in your overall package. Below is exactly what differs between the two levels, how much harder HL really is, and a framework for deciding.

## What's the same at SL and HL
Both levels are language-acquisition courses (Group 2) built around the **same five prescribed themes**:

- **Identities**
- **Experiences**
- **Human ingenuity**
- **Social organisation**
- **Sharing the planet**

SL and HL students study all five themes and are assessed on the same core skills: productive **writing** (Paper 1), **receptive** listening and reading (Paper 2), and a spoken **Individual Oral**. Crucially — and unlike academic subjects — **there is no written internal assessment in French B**. The internal component at both levels is the Individual Oral. The overlap between the two levels is large; the differences are concentrated in a few specific places.

## What HL adds
HL is not a different subject — it is the same five themes with **more demand across the board**, plus one body of content SL never touches: **two literary works**. The key differences:

- **Two literary works.** HL students study **two works of literature** in French; SL students do not study literature at all.
- **A literature-based Individual Oral.** At HL the Individual Oral is built on an **extract from one of the two literary works**. At SL the Individual Oral is based on a **visual stimulus — an image linked to a theme**.
- **Longer, more sophisticated writing.** HL Paper 1 tasks expect greater length and a **wider range of language and register**.
- **More demanding receptive texts.** HL Paper 2 uses longer and more challenging listening and reading material.

Here is the shape of the difference:

|  | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Prescribed themes | Identities, Experiences, Human ingenuity, Social organisation, Sharing the planet | Same five themes |
| Literature | None | **Two literary works** |
| Paper 1 (writing) | Produce a text type for a purpose/audience | **Longer**, wider range and register |
| Paper 2 (receptive) | Listening + reading comprehension | Same skills, **more demanding texts** |
| Individual Oral | Based on a **visual stimulus / image** | Based on an **extract from a literary work** |
| Internal assessment | **No written IA** — Individual Oral | **No written IA** — Individual Oral |
| Language demand | Solid working command | Higher fluency, range, and register |

The extra material is not padding — the two literary works, the longer writing, and the tougher receptive texts genuinely raise the bar for the fluency and range you need to demonstrate.

## How much harder is HL French B?
French B is **exposure-hard rather than content-hard** at both levels — success comes from steady contact with the language, not cramming facts. The honest difficulty gap between SL and HL is mostly about **language maturity and the literary component**. HL asks you to read two whole works in French, discuss a literary extract in your Individual Oral, and write longer, more varied responses with confident register control.

If your French is already comfortable and you enjoy reading, HL is very doable and often rewarding — reading literature deepens vocabulary and feel for the language faster than anything else. If French is a subject you find heavy going at working level, the leap to reading full literary works and speaking about them can feel steep, and that HL slot may serve you better elsewhere. ## University and diploma considerations
Language-B requirements vary far more than science requirements, so general rules matter less than the specific course:

- Some competitive courses and universities **value a higher-level modern language**, especially for languages, international relations, or programmes with a study-abroad element.
- Many degrees have **no specific language requirement**, in which case SL French B is a perfectly strong diploma choice that keeps your HL slots for subjects the course actually asks for.
- If you may study or work in a French-speaking environment, HL builds the fluency and reading stamina that pays off later.

The critical rule: **check the exact entry requirements** for the specific courses and countries you are considering, and weigh French against your other HL priorities. ## Who should take HL vs SL

**Take HL French B if you:**

- Already have a comfortable working command of French
- Enjoy reading in the language and are happy to study two literary works
- Want the fluency, range, and register that HL builds
- Are aiming at courses or paths that value a higher-level language

**Take SL French B if you:**

- Want French for breadth and balance rather than as a focus
- Are still building confidence in the language
- Would rather direct your HL energy into subjects central to your degree
- Have confirmed your target courses accept SL French B

## How to decide
1. **Start from your current French level.** Be honest about your reading and speaking comfort — HL assumes a solid base.
2. **Test your appetite for literature.** The two works define the HL experience; if reading in French appeals, that is a strong signal for HL.
3. **Read your target course requirements.** Note whether any value a higher-level language, and whether SL is accepted.
4. **Weigh your whole HL package.** You take three HLs — make sure French HL earns its place against your other priorities.
5. **Preview real content.** Skim the SL and HL courses, try some [topic practice](/ib/past-papers/french-b-hl), and feel the difference before locking in.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is there an IA in French B?
No — Language B has **no written internal assessment**. The internal component at both levels is the Individual Oral: based on a visual stimulus at SL, and on a literary extract at HL.

### How much harder is HL French B?
The main step up is the **two literary works**, a literature-based Individual Oral, longer writing, and more demanding receptive texts. If your French is comfortable and you enjoy reading, it is very achievable; if you are still building confidence, the leap is real — weigh it honestly.

### Do HL students really read two whole books?
Yes — HL French B includes **two literary works** studied in French, and one of them anchors your Individual Oral. SL students do not study literature.

### Which level is better for university?
It depends entirely on the course. Some value a higher-level language; many have no language requirement at all, making SL a strong, flexible choice. Always check published requirements for your specific courses.

### Can I take SL French B and still be good at French?
Absolutely. SL builds solid all-round French across all five themes. HL simply adds literature and higher fluency demands — SL is the right call when French is one strong part of a balanced diploma rather than a central focus.

## Bottom line
IB French B is the **same five themes with no written IA** at both levels — HL adds two literary works, a literature-based Individual Oral, longer writing, and tougher receptive texts. If your French is comfortable and you enjoy reading in the language, HL is a rewarding stretch that builds real fluency. If French is for breadth, SL delivers a strong grounding without the literary load.
