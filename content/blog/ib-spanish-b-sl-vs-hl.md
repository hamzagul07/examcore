Take **HL** if you enjoy Spanish, want a genuine step towards fluency, and don't mind reading two literary works alongside the language — or if your university plans point that way. Take **SL** if Spanish is there for breadth and diploma balance rather than as a subject you want to push hard. The honest answer is: it depends on your current level, your appetite for reading literature in Spanish, and where the subject sits in your overall plan. Below is what actually differs between the two levels, how much harder HL really is, and a framework for deciding.

## What's the same at SL and HL
Both levels sit in **Group 2, language acquisition**, and both are built around the same **five prescribed themes: identities; experiences; human ingenuity; social organisation; sharing the planet.** SL and HL students study all five themes and are assessed on the same three-part funnel:

- **Paper 1** — productive **writing**: choose one task and produce a text type (blog, email, article, speech, proposal…) for a given purpose, audience and register.
- **Paper 2** — **receptive skills**: listening comprehension and reading comprehension.
- **Individual Oral (IO)** — the internally-assessed **spoken** exam. Note there is **no written IA** in language B; the internal component is this oral.

So the skeleton is shared. What changes is the depth, the length, and — most importantly — the addition of literature at HL.

## What HL adds
HL is not a different subject; it is the same five themes taken further, plus a body of HL-only work SL students never touch. The defining additions:

- **Two literary works.** HL students study **two literary works** in Spanish. SL students do not study literature at all. This is the single biggest structural difference between the levels.
- **A literature-based Individual Oral.** At **HL the IO is based on an extract from one of the two literary works**. At **SL the IO is based on a visual stimulus** — an image linked to a theme. Same skill of spontaneous, developed speaking, but a very different starting point.
- **Longer, more sophisticated writing.** HL Paper 1 tasks are longer and expect a **wider range and register** than at SL.
- **More demanding receptive texts.** HL Paper 2 listening and reading use more challenging material.

Here is the shape of the difference:

|  | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Prescribed themes | All five | All five (same) |
| Literary works | None | **Two literary works** |
| Paper 1 (writing) | Text-type task, shorter | Text-type task, **longer, wider range/register** |
| Paper 2 (receptive) | Listening + reading | Listening + reading, **more demanding texts** |
| Individual Oral | Based on a **visual stimulus** | Based on a **literary extract** |
| Written IA | None (IO is the internal) | None (IO is the internal) |
| Reading load | Theme texts | Theme texts **plus two literary works** |

The extra literature is the headline: it adds reading, class time, and a different kind of oral preparation on top of the shared language work.

## How much harder is HL Spanish B?
Spanish B is **consistency-hard rather than content-heavy**. Unlike the sciences, there is no vast body of facts to cram — the difficulty is steady exposure and performance under pressure. HL raises the bar in two honest ways. First, the **two literary works** mean reading extended texts in Spanish and being ready to discuss them spontaneously in the oral. Second, the **longer writing and tougher listening/reading** demand a higher, more consistent level of the language rather than more memorisation.

If you already have a decent grounding in Spanish and enjoy using it, HL is very doable and genuinely rewarding — it moves you meaningfully closer to real fluency. If Spanish is a subject you tolerate rather than like, the literature and the higher performance expectations can feel heavy, and that energy may be better spent elsewhere in your diploma. As a mild, hedged observation, many English speakers find Spanish one of the more approachable Language B options thanks to its fairly transparent spelling and phonetics — but that eases the *entry*, not the HL literature workload. ## University requirements
Language requirements vary far more than science ones, so this is very course-dependent:

- **Modern-languages or Hispanic-studies degrees** — often expect a language at HL, and Spanish B HL is the natural fit. Check whether they want Spanish specifically or any language.
- **Joint-honours and area-studies courses** (Spanish with law, business, international relations) — some expect HL, many accept SL plus evidence of aptitude.
- **Most other degrees** — a language is usually valued for breadth rather than required at a set level; SL Spanish B is often perfectly sufficient.

The critical rule: **check the exact entry requirements** for the specific courses and countries you are considering. Offers differ between universities and change year to year, so read official course pages rather than relying on general advice. ## Who should take HL vs SL

**Take HL Spanish B if you:**

- Genuinely enjoy Spanish and want to push towards fluency
- Are comfortable reading two literary works and discussing them spontaneously
- Already have a solid grounding to build on
- Want or need a language at HL for your university plans

**Take SL Spanish B if you:**

- Want Spanish for breadth and diploma balance, not as a core focus
- Would rather not add two literary works to your reading load
- Are directing your HL energy into subjects more central to your degree plans
- Have confirmed your target courses accept SL Spanish B

## How to decide
1. **Start from your plan, not the subject.** List target courses and read their published IB requirements — do any ask for a language at HL?
2. **Be honest about your current level.** HL rewards students who already use Spanish comfortably; it stretches those who are still shaky on the basics.
3. **Ask how you feel about the literature.** Two literary works and a literature-based oral are the real HL commitment. If that appeals, HL is a rich choice; if it fills you with dread, SL may serve you better.
4. **Weigh your whole HL package.** You take three HLs — make sure Spanish HL earns its place against your other priorities.
5. **Preview real content.** Skim the SL and HL courses and try a few tasks before locking in.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I study literature in SL Spanish B?
No. **Only HL** studies literature — two literary works in Spanish, one of which anchors the HL Individual Oral. SL has no literature component; its oral is based on a visual stimulus instead.

### How is the Individual Oral different at SL and HL?
At **SL** the IO starts from a **visual stimulus** — an image linked to a theme. At **HL** it starts from an **extract from one of the two literary works**. Both test spontaneous, developed spoken Spanish, but HL requires you to engage with the literature.

### Is HL Spanish B a lot harder?
It is a step up mainly because of the two literary works, longer writing, and more demanding listening and reading — not because of extra factual content. It is consistency-hard, so steady practice makes it very achievable if you enjoy the language. See [is IB Spanish B hard](/blog/ib-spanish-b-hard).

### Which level do universities want?
It depends entirely on the course. Modern-languages and some joint-honours degrees may want HL; most other courses value a language for breadth and accept SL. Always confirm against each university's published requirements.

### Can I take SL and still be good at Spanish?
Absolutely. SL delivers a solid, usable grounding in the language across all five themes. HL simply adds literature, longer writing and tougher receptive texts for those who want to push further.

## Bottom line
IB Spanish B is the **same five themes and the same three-part funnel** at both levels — HL adds **two literary works**, a literature-based Individual Oral, longer writing, and more demanding receptive texts, while SL keeps a visual-stimulus oral and no literature. If you enjoy Spanish, want to push towards fluency, or need a language at HL, Higher Level is a rewarding choice. If Spanish is for breadth, SL delivers a genuine grounding without the extra reading load.
