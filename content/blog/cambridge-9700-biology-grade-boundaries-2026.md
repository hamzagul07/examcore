Cambridge A-Level Biology (9700) grade boundaries for 2026 will not be published until results day, which for the June 2026 series is 13 August 2026. These boundaries, or 'grade thresholds', are the minimum raw marks you need to achieve a certain grade (A*, A, B, etc.) and are set after all marking is complete. Understanding how they work is the key to gauging your performance without having the final, official numbers.

## What are grade boundaries?
A grade boundary is the minimum raw mark a candidate needs to secure a particular grade for a component (a single paper) or for the overall qualification. For example, the boundary for a grade 'A' in Paper 2 is the lowest mark you could get on that paper and still be awarded an A. These are always published as raw marks, not percentages, because the total number of marks varies between papers. Cambridge publishes these thresholds for every exam session to ensure transparency.

## How Cambridge sets the 9700 Biology boundaries
The primary goal for Cambridge International is to maintain a consistent standard from one year to the next. A grade 'A' in 2026 should represent the same level of achievement as a grade 'A' in 2025 or 2024. To achieve this, grade boundaries are set *after* marking is complete and a detailed statistical analysis of student performance has been conducted. If a paper is deemed more difficult than in the previous session, the grade boundaries for it will likely be lowered. Conversely, if a paper proves to be more straightforward, the boundaries will be raised to ensure the standard for each grade is upheld.

## Understanding the components of A-Level Biology (9700)
Your final A-Level Biology grade is an aggregate of several components, each with its own weighting. The syllabus is composed of Paper 1 (Multiple Choice), Paper 2 (AS Level Structured Questions), Paper 4 (A2 Level Structured Questions), and a practical paper—either Paper 3 (Advanced Practical Skills) or Paper 5 (Planning, Analysis and Evaluation). Each of these papers has its own grade boundary set each session, reflecting its specific level of difficulty. You can find more detail on the structure and weighting of these papers on our main [9700 A-Level Biology page](/subjects/9700).

## Why you can't just use last year's boundaries
While it is tempting to look at the June 2025 boundaries and assume the 2026 ones will be identical, this is a flawed approach. As explained above, the precise boundaries are a reaction to the difficulty of the specific exam papers in a given session and the performance of the student cohort. A particularly challenging set of questions on Paper 4, for instance, will almost certainly lead to a lower grade boundary for that component compared to a year with a more accessible paper. Past boundaries are a guide, not a guarantee.

## Frequently asked questions

### When will the official 9700 Biology grade boundaries for 2026 be released?
They are released on results day itself, never before. For the June 2026 examination series, this will be 13 August 2026. Cambridge publishes the component and overall thresholds on their public website at the same time that schools and centres can access student results, ensuring a fair and transparent process.

### Is it true that a certain percentage of students get an A*?
No, Cambridge does not operate a 'norm-referenced' system where a fixed quota of students gets each grade. The system is 'criterion-referenced', meaning your grade is determined by whether your work meets the required standard for that grade. While statistics are used to maintain standards over time, there isn't a pre-set limit on success.

### How important is using the exact keywords from the mark scheme?
It is critically important. Cambridge A-Level Biology marking is precise and rewards the use of correct scientific language. Examiners are instructed to look for specific keywords and phrases. An answer that is conceptually correct but lacks these key terms will often fail to score marks. This is why practising with mark schemes is essential.

### Do the grade boundaries for Paper 3 (Advanced Practical Skills) differ a lot from Paper 5 (Planning, Analysis and Evaluation)?
Yes, they often do. Although both are practical-based papers, they assess different skill sets and have unique structures and mark totals. Their relative difficulty can vary significantly from one session to another, so their grade boundaries are set independently. Always check the threshold for the specific paper you have taken.
