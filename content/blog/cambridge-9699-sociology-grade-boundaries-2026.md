The official grade boundaries for the Cambridge International A-Level Sociology (9699) 2026 exam series will be published on results day, which for the June 2026 session is 13 August 2026. These boundaries, or 'grade thresholds', are the minimum raw marks you need to achieve each grade and are determined *after* all papers have been marked. They are not known in advance, but you can estimate your potential grade by using data from previous exam sessions.

## What are grade boundaries?
A grade boundary is the minimum raw mark a candidate needs to secure a particular grade (A, B, C, etc.) for a specific paper or for the overall qualification. For A-Level Sociology, Cambridge sets separate boundaries for each component paper (e.g., Paper 1, Paper 2, Paper 3, and Paper 4) as well as an overall boundary for the full A-Level. These are not pre-set targets; they are calculated by senior examiners after the marking period is complete to ensure fairness across different exam sessions.

## Why grade boundaries change each year
Grade boundaries are not fixed because the difficulty of exam papers can vary slightly from one year to the next. To ensure that it is no more or less difficult to achieve a grade A in 2026 than it was in 2025 or 2024, Cambridge adjusts the boundaries. If a paper is deemed slightly more challenging than the previous year's, the grade boundaries may be lowered. Conversely, a more straightforward paper might have slightly higher boundaries. This process maintains the **standard** of the qualification over time, meaning your grade reflects your ability, not the specific paper you sat.

## How 9699 Sociology papers are marked
Understanding the marking process is key to understanding how your raw score is generated. A-Level Sociology essays and shorter questions are marked using a 'levels of response' system. Examiners award marks based on the quality of your sociological knowledge, your ability to apply concepts to the question, and the depth of your analysis and evaluation. To reach the higher mark bands and secure the top grades, you must demonstrate strong conceptual understanding and consistently support your arguments with evidence, including referencing named **theorists** and relevant studies. Your final raw mark for each paper is the sum of marks awarded across all questions.

## Frequently asked questions

### Will the 2026 boundaries be higher or lower than last year's?
It is impossible to know for sure. The boundaries are determined by the difficulty of the specific exam papers in the 2026 session and the overall performance of all students taking the exam. They are adjusted to maintain a consistent standard, so any change will simply reflect the challenge of the paper.

### What is the 'overall qualification' boundary?
This is the total mark needed across all your papers combined to get your final A-Level grade. Cambridge calculates this by adding the component marks, applying a weighting factor where necessary. Your final grade (e.g., 'A' or 'B') is determined by whether your total aggregate mark meets this overall threshold.

### Has Cambridge returned to pre-pandemic grading standards?
Yes. Cambridge confirmed that the grading standard returned to the pre-pandemic level from the June 2023 series onwards. This means the standard applied to the 2026 exams will be comparable to that of 2019 and earlier years, making historical grade boundaries from those years a reliable guide for estimation.

### How is the A* grade awarded?
The A* grade is not awarded for individual components, only for the overall A-Level qualification. To achieve an A*, a candidate must first achieve a grade A overall. Additionally, they must achieve a very high standard in their A2 components (Paper 3 and Paper 4), as calculated by Cambridge's aggregation method.

## Bottom line
The exact 2026 grade boundaries for 9699 Sociology remain unknown until results day. Your focus should not be on guessing these numbers, but on maximising your raw marks.
