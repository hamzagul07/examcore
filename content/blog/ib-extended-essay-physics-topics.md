A strong Physics Extended Essay research question is **quantitative, testable, and narrow**: it isolates one physical relationship you can either measure experimentally or model rigorously from established equations, then compares your results against theory with honest uncertainties. The best questions take the shape *"To what extent does [variable] affect [measurable quantity] of [system], and how well does this match [physical model]?"* — a question where you can already picture the graph, the controlled variables, and the error bars before you begin. This post is a topic-selection bank for the Physics EE; for the criteria mechanics and word-count strategy it links to the full EE guide rather than repeating them.

## Physics EE vs Physics IA — what actually differs

Both ask for an original investigation, but the scale and expectation are different. The [Physics IA](/blog/ib-physics-ia-guide) is a roughly ten-hour investigation worth a fifth of your Physics grade, marked internally against the science criteria. The Extended Essay is a **4,000-word independent research essay** in a subject of your choice, marked externally against the generic EE criteria, and — with TOK — worth up to **3 bonus diploma points**.

The practical consequence: the EE demands more depth of physics, a longer literature-grounded argument, and a genuinely sustained analysis. A bounce-height experiment that would earn full IA marks is usually too thin for an EE unless you push the modelling or the data analysis considerably further. Think "publishable mini-study," not "extended lab report."

## What makes a strong Physics EE research question

Every Physics EE that scores well shares four features:

- **Testable.** You can either collect quantitative data or model a system from established physics equations — not just describe a phenomenon in words.
- **Narrow.** One relationship, one system, a defined range. "How do black holes work" is a reading list; "How does the aspect ratio of a rectangular fin affect its cooling rate" is an EE.
- **Physics-rich.** The underlying physics is non-trivial. A relationship a GCSE student could predict in one line will not sustain 4,000 words of critical analysis.
- **Uncertainty-friendly.** You can estimate uncertainty in each measurement, propagate it into derived quantities, and use it honestly in your evaluation.

The trap is a question that is either too grand to test or too trivial to analyse. Aim for the narrow band in between where real physics meets accessible equipment.

## How to narrow a broad interest into a research question

Start from something you genuinely find interesting, then funnel it until one variable moves and one model applies:

1. **Broad interest:** aerodynamics.
2. **Physical phenomenon:** drag on a falling body.
3. **Independent variable:** cross-sectional area of a paper cone.
4. **Dependent (measurable) quantity:** terminal velocity.
5. **Model to test:** drag force proportional to velocity squared.
6. **RQ:** *How does the cross-sectional area of a paper cone affect its terminal velocity, and how well does the result agree with a quadratic drag model?*

Run this funnel on paper for two or three interests, then choose the one with the cleanest expected graph, the least awkward equipment, and the richest theory to argue against. It saves you from abandoning a topic weeks in when the physics turns out to be trivial or the kit is unavailable.

## Example research questions: too broad to sharpened

The single most common examiner complaint is a question that is too wide. Here is how vague ideas become defensible RQs — notice each sharpened version names a variable, a system, a range, and a model to test.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ |
| --- | --- |
| How does temperature affect resistance? | To what extent does temperature (20–80 °C) affect the resistance of a tungsten filament, and how well does the data fit a linear temperature-coefficient model? |
| How do pendulums work? | How does the amplitude of a simple pendulum affect its period, and at what angle does the small-angle approximation measurably break down? |
| What makes a good speaker? | How does the length of an open air column affect its fundamental resonant frequency, and how closely does it follow the inverse-length relationship? |
| How does gravity work? | How does the drop height of a steel sphere in glycerol affect its terminal velocity, and how well does Stokes' law predict the viscosity obtained? |
| Are solar panels efficient? | How does the angle of incidence of light affect the output power of a photovoltaic cell, and how well does the trend match a cosine (Lambert) model? |
| How does heat escape a house? | How does insulation thickness affect the cooling rate of a heated container, and does the data support Newton's law of cooling? |

Each sharpened RQ produces numbers you can plot, a theoretical curve to compare against, and something concrete to discuss when your data and the model disagree.

## Methodology and pitfalls

A Physics EE lives or dies on method. Whether you run an experiment or a data-analysis/modelling study, plan these before you start:

- **Controlled variables.** Name every quantity you hold fixed and how you fix it. A single uncontrolled variable can invalidate the whole argument.
- **Range and spacing.** Span a wide enough range with well-spaced points to reveal the relationship above your scatter — not two clusters at the extremes.
- **Repeats and uncertainty.** Repeat readings to average and estimate random uncertainty; identify systematic errors; propagate uncertainty into any derived quantity and show it as error bars.
- **Linearisation.** Where theory predicts a power law or exponential, transform it to a straight line — the gradient then hands you a physical constant you can quote with an uncertainty and compare to the accepted value.

The pitfalls examiners see repeatedly:

- **A question needing inaccessible equipment** — particle accelerators, ultra-high vacuums, or gear your school simply does not have. Choose a system you can actually measure.
- **Trivial physics** where the answer is obvious and there is nothing to analyse over 4,000 words.
- **A data-free "essay"** that reviews a topic without your own investigation or model — the Physics EE expects original analysis, not a report.
- **Ignoring uncertainty**, then claiming a "perfect" match to theory. Honest, quantitative error analysis scores far higher than a suspiciously clean result.

A pure secondary-data study can work well — modelling planetary orbits, analysing a published dataset — provided you do genuine physics analysis rather than summarising other people's conclusions.

## How the Extended Essay is marked

The EE is marked externally against **five criteria** totalling roughly **34 marks**, converted to a letter grade from **A to E**:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp research question and a sound, subject-appropriate methodology.
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** accurate, well-deployed physics grounded in real sources.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis, argument, and evaluation — where your uncertainty discussion earns its keep.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, citation, figures, and staying within the 4,000-word limit.
- **E — Engagement:** your reflections in the RPPF (Reflections on Planning and Progress Form).

Your EE grade combines with TOK to award **up to 3 bonus diploma points**. You will work with a **subject-qualified supervisor**, and choosing Physics means your supervisor should be a physics teacher. ## Frequently asked questions

### Does my Physics EE have to be an experiment?

No. An experiment is common, but a rigorous data-analysis or modelling study grounded in physics equations scores just as well — provided you do original quantitative analysis rather than summarising a topic. The key is testing a model against evidence, whatever the source of that evidence.

### How narrow should the research question be?

Narrow enough that you can name one independent variable, one measurable dependent quantity, a defined range, and a physical model to test — and picture the graph before you start. If you cannot draw the expected graph in advance, keep narrowing.

### Do I need advanced lab equipment?

No, and avoid RQs that demand it. Smartphone sensors, standard school apparatus, and published datasets are all legitimate. Examiners reward a well-controlled investigation with honest uncertainties over an ambitious one you cannot actually measure.

### How important is uncertainty analysis?

Central. Propagating uncertainty and using it to judge whether your data supports the model is exactly where Criterion C (critical thinking) marks are won. A "perfect" result with no error discussion looks less credible, not more.

### Should my EE topic match my HL subject?

It is recommended but not required — what you need is a subject-qualified supervisor. Choosing Physics for your EE while taking Physics HL means your strongest teacher can supervise and your deeper knowledge shows in Criterion B.

## Bottom line

Pick a physics phenomenon you find genuinely interesting, funnel it down to one measurable relationship, and choose the version where real physics meets equipment you actually have. Keep the range wide, the uncertainties honest, and the model explicit so you have something concrete to argue when data and theory disagree.
