A strong Economics Extended Essay takes **one specific market or macroeconomic question**, applies **economic theory and diagrams** to it, and — ideally — brings in some **primary data** you gathered yourself. The best topics are narrow enough that you can name the theory and the diagram before you write a word: a price floor, a tax wedge, price elasticity in a local market, a monopoly's pricing power. If your idea reads like a business report or a current-affairs opinion piece, it is not yet an Economics EE. Below are example research questions — including a "too broad → sharpened" contrast — plus how to narrow your own, the methodology examiners reward, and how the essay is marked.

This post is about *choosing and framing* an Economics EE. For the mechanics that apply to every subject — the 4,000-word limit, criteria A–E, the RPPF — see the [IB Extended Essay guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-complete-guide).

## How the Economics EE differs from the Economics IA

They feel similar because both apply theory to the real world, but the scale and skill are different:

- The **IA** is a portfolio of three short commentaries (~800 words each) built on recent news articles, each attaching one diagram to one event. See the [IB Economics IA guide](/blog/ib-economics-ia-guide).
- The **EE** is a single **4,000-word research essay** answering one focused research question. It expects **sustained argument**, a **methodology** (often including primary data), and depth an 800-word commentary cannot reach.

The IA rewards a clean diagram and a quick evaluation. The EE rewards a genuine *investigation*: a question, evidence gathered to answer it, theory applied throughout, and a reasoned conclusion that weighs limitations.

## How to choose and narrow a research question

The single biggest EE mistake in Economics is a question that is really a topic. "Inflation in Argentina" is a topic; it has no answer. Narrow along three axes:

- **A specific market or agent.** One firm, one product, one local market — "the market for artisan coffee in [town]", not "the coffee industry".
- **A specific theory or mechanism.** Elasticity, externalities, price discrimination, a price ceiling, market structure. The theory should be visible in the question.
- **A measurable effect.** "To what extent did X affect Y?" gives you something to investigate and evaluate, rather than describe.

Aim for a question you could answer in a paragraph as a hypothesis, then spend 4,000 words testing. If you cannot picture the **diagram** the essay will lean on, keep narrowing.

## Example research questions

Use these as **templates for the level of focus to aim for**, not questions to copy. The strongest EEs are tied to a market you can actually gather data on — a local firm, a nearby market, your own survey.

| Too broad ❌ | Sharpened ✅ | Theory / diagram |
|---|---|---|
| How does tax affect consumers? | To what extent did the 2024 sugar tax change consumer demand for soft drinks at three supermarkets in [town]? | Indirect tax on a demerit good; tax wedge, PED |
| Why do coffee shops charge different prices? | To what extent does price discrimination explain the pricing of [café chain] across two [city] neighbourhoods? | Price discrimination; consumer surplus |
| Is the minimum wage good or bad? | What was the effect of the 2025 minimum-wage rise on staffing decisions at small restaurants in [area]? | Price floor in a labour market; surplus of labour |
| How does supply and demand work? | How price-elastic is demand for parking in [city centre] following the [date] tariff increase? | Price elasticity of demand; revenue |
| Are monopolies bad? | To what extent does [firm]'s market power in [local market] raise prices above the competitive level? | Market structure; monopoly diagram |
| Does the exchange rate affect trade? | To what extent did the [currency] depreciation in 2024 affect export revenue for [local exporter]? | Exchange rates; forex demand and supply |

Notice the pattern: each sharpened question names a **place, a time, and a mechanism**, and points at a diagram. That is what turns a topic into an investigation.

## Subject-specific methodology and pitfalls

An Economics EE is **theory-driven** — economics is the lens, not decoration. To hit the top bands:

- **Apply economic theory throughout**, not just in an introduction. Every section should advance an argument built on models.
- **Use diagrams to analyse**, not to illustrate. A well-explained tax wedge or AD/AS shift that carries part of the argument beats three decorative sketches.
- **Gather primary data where you can.** A survey of local consumers, price observations across shops, or an interview with a firm gives you something original to analyse — and material for genuine evaluation of reliability.
- **Support with recent, credible secondary data** — official statistics, firm reports, reputable news — but keep the **theory timeless** even as the data is current.
- **Evaluate honestly.** Sample size, assumptions behind your model, ceteris paribus, short-run versus long-run. Weighing limitations is where critical-thinking marks live.

Common pitfalls that sink Economics EEs:

- **A business report in disguise.** If the essay is about a firm's strategy with no economic theory or diagrams, it belongs in Business Management, not Economics.
- **Current-affairs commentary.** Describing a policy or news event without applying models is an extended IA, not an EE.
- **A question too broad to answer** — no place, no time, no measurable effect.
- **Data with no analysis.** A survey is worthless unless economic theory explains the result.
- **Old or generic data** where a specific, recent, local dataset was available.

To sharpen the underlying theory and diagrams before you commit, work through the free [Economics HL course](/ib/courses/economics-hl) or [Economics SL course](/ib/courses/economics-sl), and if you are chasing top marks read [how to get a 7 in IB Economics](/blog/ib-economics-how-to-get-a-7).

## How the Economics EE is marked

The EE is **externally marked out of 34** and graded **A–E**, on five criteria that apply across every subject:

- **A — Focus and method:** a sharp, well-framed research question and a sound methodology (this is where your data-gathering plan is judged).
- **B — Knowledge and understanding:** command of relevant economic theory, terminology, and context.
- **C — Critical thinking:** analysis and evaluation — applying models to your evidence and weighing limitations.
- **D — Presentation:** structure, diagrams, citation, and staying within the 4,000 words.
- **E — Engagement:** assessed through your **RPPF** reflections with your supervisor.

Together with TOK, a strong EE can contribute **up to 3 bonus diploma points**. The exact interpretation for Economics — how diagrams, data, and evaluation are weighted within each criterion — is set out in the current subject-specific EE guidance, so **confirm the finer points with your supervisor**. For how the bands work more generally, see [IB markbands explained](/blog/ib-markbands-explained) and [IB command terms explained](/blog/ib-command-terms-explained).

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I need primary data for an Economics EE?

It is not strictly required, but it is strongly rewarded. Original data — a survey, price observations, or an interview — gives you something genuine to analyse and to evaluate for reliability, which feeds directly into the critical-thinking criterion. If primary data is impractical, use specific, recent secondary data on a narrow market. Check the current expectation with your supervisor.

### How do I keep it from becoming a Business Management essay?

Lead with **theory and diagrams**, not the company. An Economics EE asks *why the market behaves this way* using models — elasticity, market structure, externalities — even when a firm is the setting. If your argument is about strategy or management decisions rather than economic mechanisms, it has drifted into Business Management. See the [Business Management EE topics guide](/blog/ib-extended-essay-business-management-topics) for the contrast.

### Micro or macro — which is easier to do well?

**Micro** is usually easier to keep focused: a single market, one diagram, and often gatherable primary data. Macro questions can work but are harder to narrow and to isolate cause and effect, since so many variables move at once. If in doubt, a tightly defined micro market is the safer path to the top bands.

### Does the EE subject have to match my HL Economics?

It is recommended but not required — what you need is a **subject-qualified supervisor** in Economics. Doing it in a subject you take at HL usually means stronger theory to draw on, which helps criterion B.

### How recent does my data need to be?

The **theory is timeless**, but your evidence should be current — recent statistics, a firm's latest figures, or data you collected yourself. Fresh, specific data signals a real investigation; recycled generic figures signal a rushed one.

## Bottom line

A great Economics EE is not a clever topic — it is a **narrow market or question you can attach real theory, a clear diagram, and (ideally) your own data to**. Name the theory and the diagram before you write, keep economics as the lens rather than decoration, and evaluate your evidence honestly.
